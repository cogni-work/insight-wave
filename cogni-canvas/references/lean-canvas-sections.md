# Lean Canvas Section Reference

## The 9 Sections

A Lean Canvas captures a business hypothesis on a single page. Each section has a specific purpose and common pitfalls.

### 1. Problem (Top 1-3 problems)

**Purpose**: Define the pain points the business addresses.

**Quality criteria**:
- Specific and measurable where possible (e.g., "10 hours/week on manual reporting" not "reporting is slow")
- Distinct problems, not variations of the same one
- Validated or at least observable in the target segment
- Framed from the customer's perspective, not the builder's

**Common pitfalls**:
- Listing solutions disguised as problems ("They don't have our product")
- Too abstract ("Inefficiency in workflows")
- Too many problems (signal lack of focus)

**Guiding questions**:
- What are customers doing today instead?
- What does this problem cost them (time, money, reputation)?
- How often does this problem occur?

---

### 2. Customer Segments

**Purpose**: Define who has the problem and will pay for a solution.

**Quality criteria**:
- Segments are distinct (different pain, different buying behavior)
- Primary segment identified (beachhead market)
- Each segment has: pain description, value statement, budget indication, market size estimate
- Segments ordered by priority (primary first)

**Common pitfalls**:
- Too broad ("SMEs" without qualification)
- No prioritization between segments
- Missing budget/willingness-to-pay signal
- Segments that overlap significantly

**Guiding questions**:
- Who feels this problem most acutely?
- Who has budget authority to buy?
- Which segment can you reach most easily?
- What's the minimum viable segment to start with?

---

### 3. Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

**Purpose**: Single clear compelling message that states why the solution is different and worth attention.

**Quality criteria**:
- One sentence, not a paragraph
- Addresses the primary segment's #1 pain
- Differentiates from alternatives (not just competitors)
- Specific enough to be falsifiable
- Segment-specific variants provided when segments have different pain points

**Common pitfalls**:
- Feature list disguised as UVP
- Buzzword soup ("AI-powered next-gen platform")
- No differentiation ("Better, faster, cheaper")
- Trying to address all segments in one statement

**Guiding questions**:
- If a customer reads this in 8 seconds, do they understand what's different?
- Would a competitor's customer switch based on this statement?
- Can you deliver on this promise today (or within 90 days)?

---

### 4. Solution

**Purpose**: Define the top features or capabilities that address the problems.

**Quality criteria**:
- Maps clearly to problems listed in Section 1
- Organized in layers or components (technology, knowledge, service, etc.)
- Each component has a clear scope and boundary
- Feasible with current or near-term resources
- Not over-specified (canvas is a hypothesis, not a spec)

**Common pitfalls**:
- Feature laundry list without structure
- Solution looking for a problem
- Over-engineering at canvas stage
- No mapping back to customer problems

**Guiding questions**:
- Which capabilities address the #1 problem for the #1 segment?
- What's the minimum solution that delivers the UVP?
- What can be deferred to later iterations?

---

### 5. Channels

**Purpose**: How to reach and deliver value to customer segments.

**Quality criteria**:
- At least one channel per segment
- Mix of owned, earned, and paid channels
- Channels matched to segment behavior (where do they already look?)
- Prioritized by cost-effectiveness and reach

**Common pitfalls**:
- Only listing aspirational channels ("viral growth")
- No channel for the primary segment
- Ignoring existing relationships and networks
- Channel requires budget the business doesn't have

**Guiding questions**:
- Where does the primary segment look for solutions today?
- What channels can you activate in the next 30 days?
- Which channels compound over time?

---

### 6. Revenue Streams

**Purpose**: Define how the business makes money.

**Quality criteria**:
- Each stream tied to a solution component or segment
- Pricing model specified (subscription, project, per-seat, etc.)
- Pricing assumptions stated (even if rough)
- Revenue timing considered (upfront vs. recurring)

**Common pitfalls**:
- "Freemium" without a clear conversion path
- Pricing pulled from thin air with no anchor
- Too many revenue streams for an early stage
- No recurring revenue component

**Guiding questions**:
- What would customers pay for today (not in 2 years)?
- What's the anchor price in the customer's mind?
- Is there a recurring revenue component?
- What's the minimum price that makes the unit economics work?

---

### 7. Cost Structure

**Purpose**: List the main costs to operate the business.

**Quality criteria**:
- Fixed vs. variable costs distinguished
- Largest cost drivers identified
- Realistic for current stage (not at-scale costs)
- Includes hidden costs (API fees, compliance, support)

**Common pitfalls**:
- Only listing development costs
- Ignoring customer acquisition costs
- Underestimating operational costs
- Not accounting for API/cloud costs that scale with usage

**Guiding questions**:
- What are the top 3 cost drivers?
- Which costs scale with customers vs. which are fixed?
- What's the monthly burn rate to operate?

---

### 8. Key Metrics

**Purpose**: Define the numbers that indicate business health and progress.

**Quality criteria**:
- 3-5 metrics maximum
- Mix of leading (predictive) and lagging (outcome) indicators
- Each metric is actionable (you can do something to change it)
- Tied to the specific business model, not generic vanity metrics
- Includes at least one metric per: acquisition, activation, revenue

**Common pitfalls**:
- Vanity metrics ("page views", "downloads")
- Too many metrics (loss of focus)
- No leading indicators (only measuring outcomes after the fact)
- Metrics that can't be measured with current tools

**Guiding questions**:
- What single metric would tell you the business is working?
- What leading indicator predicts revenue 90 days out?
- What metric would make you pivot if it stayed flat?

---

### 9. Unfair Advantage

**Purpose**: Something that cannot be easily copied or bought.

**Quality criteria**:
- Genuinely hard to replicate (not just "first mover")
- Compounds over time
- Specific to this team/business
- Honest — "none yet" is better than fabricated advantages

**Common pitfalls**:
- Claiming "technology" as unfair advantage (easily replicated)
- Listing features rather than structural advantages
- "First mover" (rarely a real advantage)
- Confusing competitive advantage with unfair advantage

**Real unfair advantages include**:
- Insider knowledge / domain expertise
- Network effects
- Existing community or audience
- Proprietary data
- Regulatory or structural barriers
- Personal authority / reputation in the space
- Dream team with unique combination of skills

**Guiding questions**:
- What do you have that a well-funded competitor couldn't replicate in 6 months?
- What compounds the longer you operate?
- What would be lost if the founding team left?

---

## Section Interdependencies

Sections are not independent — they form a coherent story:

| If you change... | Also review... |
|---|---|
| Problem | Customer Segments (do they still have this problem?), Solution (does it still address it?), UVP (does it still differentiate?) |
| Customer Segments | Problem (is it their #1 pain?), Channels (can you reach them?), Revenue (will they pay?) |
| UVP | Problem (does it address it?), Solution (can you deliver?), Unfair Advantage (why you?) |
| Solution | Problem (does it solve it?), Cost Structure (can you afford it?), Revenue (does pricing match?) |
| Revenue Streams | Solution (what are they paying for?), Customer Segments (will they pay this?), Cost Structure (are margins viable?) |

## Canvas Maturity Stages

| Stage | Characteristics | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Draft** | Many "?" sections, rough ideas | Fill all 9 sections with hypotheses |
| **Hypothesis** | All sections filled, untested | Identify riskiest assumptions |
| **Validated** | Key assumptions tested with evidence | Refine based on market feedback |
| **Evolved** | Multiple versions, data-driven changes | Track what changed and why |
