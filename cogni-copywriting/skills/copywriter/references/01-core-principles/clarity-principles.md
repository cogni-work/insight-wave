# Clarity Principles (Wolf-Schneider)

<context>
You are applying Wolf-Schneider's clarity principles to business writing. These principles govern sentence structure, word choice, and information placement. They apply to every deliverable type and are loaded as Tier 1 for every copywriting task.

Your goal: Make every sentence immediately comprehensible on first reading. The reader should never need to re-read a sentence to understand it.
</context>

## Decision Framework

Use this decision tree when evaluating any sentence or paragraph for clarity:

```
Is the main point in the main clause?
  NO  --> Restructure: promote the buried point to the main clause
  YES --> Is the sentence under 20 words?
            NO  --> Split into two sentences, or cut modifiers
            YES --> Are all terms concrete and specific?
                      NO  --> Replace abstractions with measurable specifics
                      YES --> Is jargon present that a non-specialist would miss?
                                NO  --> Sentence passes clarity check
                                YES --> Replace jargon with plain equivalent
```

## Principle 1: Main Things in Main Clauses

The most important information belongs in the grammatically strongest position: the main clause. Subordinate clauses, prepositional phrases, and parenthetical insertions weaken and bury your point.

**Detection pattern:** If the sentence's core message lives inside a "because", "although", "due to", "in light of", or "considering" clause, the structure is wrong.

### Transformation Examples

**Buried lead (subordinate clause hides the point):**

Before: "Due to difficult weather conditions caused by an unexpected cold snap during the night, rescue workers were unable to reach the stranded hikers in time."

Problem: The actual news (rescue workers failed to reach hikers) is buried after 14 words of context.

After: "An unexpected cold snap prevented rescue workers from reaching the hikers in time."

Why this works: The subject (cold snap) and the action (prevented) appear immediately. The reader gets the point in the first 5 words.

---

**Stacked prepositional phrases:**

Before: "In accordance with the findings of the review committee regarding the performance metrics of Q3, we have decided to restructure the sales division."

Problem: Four prepositional phrases ("in accordance with", "of the review committee", "regarding the performance metrics", "of Q3") delay the main point by 19 words.

After: "We are restructuring the sales division. The Q3 performance review supports this decision."

Why this works: Two short sentences. The decision comes first. The justification follows separately.

---

**Nominalization chain hiding the action:**

Before: "The implementation of the new customer relationship management system resulted in the improvement of response times and the reduction of ticket backlog."

Problem: Three nominalizations ("implementation", "improvement", "reduction") hide who did what. No human actor appears.

After: "The new CRM system cut response times by 40% and halved the ticket backlog."

Why this works: The system is the actor. "Cut" and "halved" are concrete verbs with measurable outcomes.

### Application Rule

When you encounter a sentence where the main point appears after word 15 or later, restructure it. Move the core message to the sentence's opening position.

## Principle 2: Short Sentences

Long sentences overload working memory. Each sentence should deliver one idea completely before the next begins.

**Targets by deliverable type:**

| Deliverable | Average sentence length | Maximum per sentence | Rhythm range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Memos | 12-18 words | 22 words | 8-22 words |
| Emails | 12-18 words | 22 words | 8-22 words |
| Briefs | 15-20 words | 25 words | 10-25 words |
| Reports | 15-20 words | 25 words | 10-25 words |
| Proposals | 15-20 words | 25 words | 10-25 words |
| Executive summaries | 12-18 words | 22 words | 8-22 words |

**Sentence rhythm matters.** Do not write every sentence at exactly 15 words. Vary between short punchy sentences (8-12 words) and longer explanatory ones (18-25 words). Monotone sentence length creates dull prose.

### Splitting Long Sentences

**Before (38 words):**
"The quarterly review revealed that our customer satisfaction scores have declined by 12 points since January, which management attributes to the transition to the new support platform that was deployed without adequate staff training."

**Step-by-step split:**

1. Identify the distinct ideas: (a) satisfaction scores dropped, (b) the cause is the new platform, (c) training was inadequate
2. Give each idea its own sentence
3. Connect them with logical sequence, not conjunctions

**After (3 sentences, 13/12/9 words):**
"Customer satisfaction scores dropped 12 points since January. The new support platform caused the decline. Staff received inadequate training before deployment."

### When to Keep a Longer Sentence

A sentence can exceed 20 words if it contains a natural list or parallel structure:

Acceptable (24 words): "The restructuring plan addresses three priorities: reducing overhead by 20%, consolidating regional offices, and retraining 150 customer support staff."

This works because the colon introduces a scannable list within a single coherent thought.

## Principle 3: Concrete Language Over Abstract Language

Abstract language forces the reader to guess your meaning. Concrete language creates shared understanding on first reading.

### The Abstraction Ladder

Think of language on a ladder from abstract (top) to concrete (bottom). Always climb down the ladder.

```
ABSTRACT (avoid)    "significant improvement in performance"
     |              "better results"
     |              "higher output"
     |              "20% more units produced"
CONCRETE (prefer)   "Factory B produced 2,400 units in March, up from 2,000 in February"
```

### Transformation Lookup Table

| Abstract term | Concrete replacement | Context note |
|---|---|---|
| "significant increase" | "doubled", "up 40%", "grew from 50 to 85" | Always use the actual number if available |
| "soon" | "by March 15", "within two weeks", "next Tuesday" | Pin to a specific date or timeframe |
| "many" | "127", "over 300", "six out of ten" | Use exact count or bounded range |
| "large" | "500 employees", "12 offices", "$4.2M" | Quantify the dimension that matters |
| "improved" | "cut from 5 days to 2 days", "reduced errors by 60%" | State the before/after or the delta |
| "issues" | "login failures", "late shipments", "billing errors" | Name the specific issue type |
| "items" | "laptops", "support tickets", "contract renewals" | Name the actual objects |
| "stakeholders" | "the CFO and VP of Sales", "regional managers" | Name the people or roles |
| "optimize" | "cut costs by 15%", "reduce processing from 4 hours to 45 minutes" | State what optimization means in measurable terms |
| "leverage" | "use", "apply", "build on" | Replace with the plain verb that fits |
| "synergies" | "shared customer base", "combined logistics network" | Name the specific advantage |
| "solution" | "the new CRM platform", "automated billing system" | Name the actual product or approach |
| "resources" | "three developers and $200K budget", "two trucks" | Specify the actual resources |
| "alignment" | "the marketing and sales teams now share the same quarterly targets" | Describe what aligned means in practice |

### Full Paragraph Transformation

**Before (abstract):**
"The organization is experiencing challenges in multiple operational areas. Various stakeholders have expressed concerns regarding the current trajectory. We recommend implementing strategic initiatives to address these issues and drive meaningful improvements across key performance indicators."

Problems: "challenges" (which ones?), "multiple operational areas" (which?), "various stakeholders" (who?), "concerns" (about what?), "strategic initiatives" (what specifically?), "meaningful improvements" (how much?), "key performance indicators" (which metrics?).

**After (concrete):**
"Shipping delays and billing errors cost us $340K last quarter. The CFO and three regional managers flagged these problems in September. We recommend two fixes: automate invoice processing (saves $200K/year) and switch to a regional distribution model (cuts delivery time from 5 days to 2)."

Why this works: Every claim is specific. The reader knows exactly what happened, who raised it, what to do, and what it will achieve.

## Principle 4: Simple Words Over Complex Words

Choose the shortest, most common word that accurately conveys your meaning. Do not use a complex word to sound professional. Precision and simplicity are not opposites.

### Word Replacement Table

| Complex | Simple | When to keep the complex form |
|---|---|---|
| "utilize" | "use" | Never -- "utilize" adds no precision |
| "implement" | "set up", "start", "launch" | Keep "implement" only in technical/IT contexts where it has specific meaning |
| "facilitate" | "help", "enable", "run" | Never in business prose |
| "methodology" | "method", "approach" | Keep only in academic or research contexts |
| "commence" | "start", "begin" | Never |
| "terminate" | "end", "stop", "fire" | Keep only in legal/HR contexts where it has specific meaning |
| "endeavor" | "try", "effort" | Never |
| "subsequent" | "next", "later", "after" | Never |
| "approximately" | "about", "roughly", "around" | Keep in technical writing requiring precision markers |
| "aforementioned" | "this", "that", "the" | Never |
| "notwithstanding" | "despite", "although" | Never in business prose |
| "pursuant to" | "under", "following", "per" | Keep only in legal writing |
| "in lieu of" | "instead of" | Never in business prose |
| "pertaining to" | "about", "on", "for" | Never |

**The 14-year-old test:** Read the sentence aloud. Could a smart 14-year-old understand it without asking what a word means? If not, find simpler words. This does not mean dumbing down. It means removing unnecessary complexity.

### Jargon Handling Rules

1. **Internal documents for specialists:** Domain jargon acceptable if the audience shares it. "The API latency SLA breach" is fine for an engineering team.
2. **Mixed audiences:** Define jargon on first use or replace it. "The API latency (server response time) exceeded the 200ms target."
3. **Executive audiences:** Eliminate jargon entirely. "The server is too slow. Customer-facing pages take 3 seconds to load instead of the target 0.2 seconds."
4. **External documents:** Default to zero jargon unless the audience has confirmed domain expertise.

## Integration with Messaging Frameworks

Each clarity principle maps to specific framework elements:

| Framework | Where clarity applies most | Specific technique |
|---|---|---|
| BLUF | The bottom-line sentence itself | Main point in main clause; under 20 words; concrete outcome stated |
| Pyramid | The governing thought at each level | One idea per sentence; concrete evidence at the lowest level |
| SCQA | The Complication and Answer sections | Concrete problem statement; specific, measurable solution |
| STAR | The Result section | Quantified outcome; concrete before/after comparison |
| FAB | The Benefit section | Measurable benefit in the reader's terms, not the product's terms |
| PSB | The Problem section | Specific pain point with concrete cost or impact |

## Validation Checklist

Apply this checklist during the final review pass of any deliverable. Each item is a binary pass/fail check.

- [ ] Average sentence length falls within the target range for this deliverable type (see table above)
- [ ] No sentence exceeds the maximum word count for this deliverable type
- [ ] The main point of every paragraph appears in the first or second sentence (not buried later)
- [ ] Abstract terms have been replaced with concrete, measurable specifics -- apply the Abstraction Ladder
- [ ] Vague quantifiers ("many", "several", "significant") have been replaced with numbers or bounded ranges
- [ ] Technical jargon is either eliminated or defined on first use, based on the audience
- [ ] Every sentence has a clear, identifiable subject performing a clear action
- [ ] No sentence requires re-reading to understand on first pass

**COMPRESSION EXCLUSION:** Inline citations `[PREFIX-N](url)` are evidence markers required for verification. They are not removable content. Never count citation markers toward word-count targets. Never replace inline citations with a summary footer.

## Common Failure Patterns and Fixes

### Failure 1: The "Throat-Clearing" Opening

Sentences that begin with meaningless preamble before reaching the point.

Before: "It is important to note that in light of recent developments in the competitive landscape, we need to reconsider our pricing strategy."

Fix: Delete everything before the actual statement.

After: "Competitors cut prices by 15% last quarter. We need to respond."

Detection: Look for openings like "It is important to note that", "It should be mentioned that", "As you may be aware", "In consideration of the fact that". Delete them.

### Failure 2: The Compound-Complex Labyrinth

Sentences with multiple dependent clauses chained together.

Before: "Although the team completed the audit on schedule, which was surprising given the resource constraints that had been imposed by the budget review committee, the findings, which were more extensive than anticipated, required additional analysis that could not be completed before the board meeting."

Fix: Break at each logical boundary. One idea per sentence.

After: "The team completed the audit on schedule despite tight resource constraints. The findings were more extensive than expected. Additional analysis will continue past the board meeting."

Detection: Count conjunctions and relative pronouns ("which", "that", "although", "because", "while") in a single sentence. If there are three or more, split the sentence.

### Failure 3: The Abstraction Avalanche

Multiple abstract terms in sequence with no concrete anchor.

Before: "We need to implement a holistic approach to stakeholder engagement that leverages synergistic methodologies to drive transformational outcomes across the enterprise."

Fix: Ask "What would someone actually DO after reading this?" Answer that question concretely.

After: "We need to meet with each department head monthly and align their quarterly goals with the company's three strategic priorities."

Detection: Highlight every noun and verb. If more than half are abstract (cannot be photographed, counted, or directly observed), rewrite the sentence.

## Wolf-Schneider Core Rules Summary

Apply these five rules in order of priority:

1. **Main things in main clauses.** The most important information belongs in the grammatically strongest position. If your point lives in a subordinate clause, restructure.
2. **Short sentences.** Target 15-20 words average. Split at logical boundaries. Vary rhythm between 8 and 25 words.
3. **Concrete beats abstract.** "Doubled" not "significant increase." "$4.2M" not "substantial investment." "March 15" not "soon."
4. **Simple beats complex.** "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence." The plainest accurate word wins.
5. **Specific beats general.** "127 customers" not "many customers." "Login failures" not "issues." "The CFO" not "stakeholders."
