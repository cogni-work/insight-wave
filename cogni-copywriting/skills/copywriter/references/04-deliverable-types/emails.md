# Professional Emails

## Quick Reference

**Purpose:** Direct business communication requiring action, decision, or awareness
**Length:** 150-300 words (3-5 paragraphs)
**Formality:** Medium (calibrate to relationship and context)
**Audience:** Internal colleagues, external stakeholders
**Best frameworks:** BLUF (action requests), SCQA (problem-solving)
**Key principles:** Clarity, conciseness, active voice
**Critical constraint:** 60% of business emails are read on mobile. The first two lines must stand alone.

## Core Rule

Every email answers exactly one question for the reader: "What do I need to know or do?"

State that answer in the first two sentences. Everything after exists only to support it.

## Decision Logic: Choosing the Right Framework

Think step by step when selecting a framework:

1. Does the email request a specific action or approval? Use **BLUF**.
2. Does the email present a problem that needs solving? Use **SCQA**.
3. Does the email share a status update? Use **BLUF** (lead with status, then metrics).
4. Does the email deliver bad news to someone you need buy-in from? Use **SCQA** (build context before the difficult ask).

If unsure, default to BLUF. It works for 80% of business emails.

## Standard Structure

### 1. Subject Line

The subject line is the most important line in the email. It determines whether the email gets opened, and when.

**Formula:** `[Action/Status] + [Key Detail] + [Deadline if applicable]`

**Requirements:**
- 6-10 words (50 characters max for mobile preview)
- Front-load the action verb or status indicator
- Include deadline when one exists
- Signal whether action is required

**Good vs. bad subject lines:**

| Good | Bad | Why it fails |
|------|-----|--------------|
| Approve $50K Budget by Oct 30 | Budget | No action, no specificity |
| Q4 Production 15% Ahead -- No Action Required | Update | No content hint, no urgency signal |
| Meeting Rescheduled to Friday 2 PM | Meeting Change | Missing the new time |
| Security Patch Required by EOD Friday | Important -- Please Read | Clickbait with zero information |

### 2. Opening (First 1-2 sentences)

State the main point immediately using BLUF. This opening must make sense when read alone on a mobile lock screen.

**Include:**
- What the reader needs to do or know
- The deadline (if action is required)
- Why it matters (in one clause)

**Do NOT include:** greetings longer than "Hi [Name]," or any preamble about hoping the reader is well.

### 3. Body (1-3 short paragraphs)

Provide only the context the reader needs to act on the opening statement. Use bullet points when presenting three or more items.

**Paragraph rules:**
- 2-4 sentences per paragraph
- 12-18 words per sentence (shorter than memos)
- One idea per paragraph

### 4. Closing (1-2 sentences)

Make the next step frictionless. Tell the reader exactly what to do and how.

**Good closings reduce the reader's effort:**
- "Reply 'Approved' and I'll execute contracts immediately."
- "Does Friday 2-3 PM work? Calendar invite attached."
- "No action required. FYI only."

**Bad closings create ambiguity:**
- "Let me know your thoughts when you get a chance."
- "We should find a time to discuss this soon."
- Ending without clarifying whether a response is expected.

## Framework Application

### BLUF Email (Action Requests, Status Updates, Decisions)

Use BLUF when the reader needs to act, approve, or simply be informed. Lead with the bottom line.

```markdown
Subject: Approve $50K Marketing Budget by Oct 30

Hi [Name],

I need your approval for the $50K Q4 marketing budget by October 30 to meet vendor deadlines.

We negotiated favorable rates with three vendors for our holiday campaign targeting our best-performing Q3 demographic. They require commitment by month-end.

Budget breakdown:
- Digital ads: $30K (15% conversion rate in Q3)
- Content production: $15K
- Analytics tools: $5K
- Expected revenue: $200K based on Q3 performance

Reply with approval and I'll execute contracts immediately.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Why this works:** The reader knows the ask ($50K approval), the deadline (Oct 30), and the reason (vendor deadlines) within the first sentence. The bullet list makes the breakdown scannable. The closing tells the reader exactly what to do.

### SCQA Email (Problem-Solving, Complex Decisions)

Use SCQA when you need to build a brief case before presenting a recommendation. The reader needs context to understand why the ask matters.

```markdown
Subject: Warehouse Capacity Solution Needed by Nov 1

Hi [Name],

Our warehouse is at 95% capacity with orders up 40% this year. We are turning away new business and competitors are gaining share. Holiday season will worsen delays.

I recommend leasing 50,000 sq ft within 30 days ($30K/month) while planning permanent expansion for Q2 2026. This provides immediate relief before the holiday rush without committing to a $2M capital project.

Can we discuss the approval path by Nov 1?

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

**Why this works:** The situation and complication are compressed into the first paragraph. The recommendation follows immediately with cost and timeline. The reader grasps the problem and proposed solution in under 15 seconds.

### Status Update Email

Status updates use BLUF structure but signal "no action required" upfront to respect the reader's time.

```markdown
Subject: Q4 Production 15% Ahead -- No Action Required

Hi Team,

Q4 production is 15% ahead of schedule and 8% under budget. No action required.

Key metrics:
- Units produced: 12,500 (target: 10,850)
- Budget spent: $920K (budget: $1M)
- Defect rate: 1.2% (target: 1.5%)

Process improvements from September are working well. We expect to maintain this pace through year-end.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Why this works:** "No action required" in the opening lets busy readers move on. Metrics are presented as target vs. actual for instant comprehension.

## Email Type Patterns

Use these patterns as structural starting points. Each pattern answers the question: what does the reader need from this email type?

### Action Request
- **Reader needs:** What to do, by when, why
- **Structure:** BLUF (action + deadline) then brief rationale then easy response path
- **Closing pattern:** "Reply 'Approved' and I'll proceed."

### Status Update
- **Reader needs:** Current state vs. target, whether action is needed
- **Structure:** BLUF (status + "no action required" or "action needed") then key metrics then outlook
- **Closing pattern:** "No action required." or "Flag any concerns by [date]."

### Meeting Request
- **Reader needs:** Why meet, when, what to prepare
- **Structure:** Purpose and proposed time then agenda (3-5 bullets) then prep instructions
- **Closing pattern:** "Does [time] work? Calendar invite attached."

### Information Sharing
- **Reader needs:** What to know, whether to act
- **Structure:** Key information in first paragraph then why it matters to the reader then resources/links
- **Closing pattern:** "FYI only. Full details: [link]."

### Bad News / Sensitive Communication
- **Reader needs:** Context before the difficult message, clear next steps
- **Structure:** Use SCQA. Brief context then the news then what happens next then offer to discuss
- **Closing pattern:** "Happy to discuss. My calendar is open Thursday afternoon."

## Tone Calibration

Calibrate tone based on three factors: audience, relationship maturity, and urgency.

### Internal Emails
- Professional but conversational
- Use first names
- Direct requests without excessive hedging
- Match the team's communication norms

### External Emails
- Default to more formal until the relationship establishes a pattern
- Mirror the recipient's tone in subsequent exchanges
- Err toward professionalism when uncertain
- Account for cultural communication preferences

### Urgent Emails
- Add "Urgent:" prefix to subject line (use sparingly to preserve impact)
- State action and deadline in the first sentence
- Remove all non-essential content
- Keep under 100 words

**Tone spectrum with examples:**

| Context | Example Opening |
|---------|-----------------|
| Peer, internal, routine | "Hi Sarah, I need your sign-off on the Q4 budget by Friday." |
| Senior leader, internal | "Hi Dr. Chen, requesting your approval for the Q4 marketing budget ($50K) by October 30." |
| New external contact | "Dear Ms. Rodriguez, thank you for your time on our call today. I am writing to outline the next steps we discussed." |
| Established external partner | "Hi James, following up on our conversation -- here is the proposal we discussed." |

## Before/After: Applying Email Principles

### Transformation 1: Eliminating Throat-Clearing

**Before (128 words):**
```
Subject: Marketing Campaign

Hi Team,

I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out to discuss a matter
that has come to my attention regarding the upcoming marketing campaign that
we have been planning for Q4. As you may recall from our previous discussions,
we have been working diligently with several vendors to finalize the details
of the campaign, and I am pleased to report that we have made significant
progress. After careful consideration and extensive negotiations, I believe
we are now in a position to move forward with the next phase of our planning
process, which will require some important decisions to be made by the team.

Let me know your thoughts.
```

**After (62 words):**
```
Subject: Approve $50K Marketing Budget by Oct 30

Hi Team,

I need approval for the $50K Q4 marketing budget by October 30 to lock in
vendor rates.

Budget breakdown:
- Digital ads: $30K (15% conversion rate)
- Content production: $15K
- Analytics: $5K
- Expected revenue: $200K

Reply with approval and I'll execute contracts immediately.

Best,
[Name]
```

**What changed and why:**
- Subject line: vague "Marketing Campaign" became specific action with deadline
- Opening: 3 sentences of pleasantries became 1 sentence with the ask
- Body: narrative prose became scannable bullet list
- Closing: "Let me know your thoughts" became a specific, low-effort response path
- Word count: cut 52% while adding more useful information

### Transformation 2: Splitting a Multi-Topic Email

**Before (one email covering three topics):**
```
Subject: Several Items

Hi Manager,

First, I wanted to update you on the Q4 production numbers. We are ahead by
15%. Also, I need to reschedule our Wednesday meeting to Friday. And finally,
could you approve the new vendor contract by end of week?

Thanks
```

**After (three separate emails):**

Email 1:
```
Subject: Q4 Production 15% Ahead -- No Action Required
```

Email 2:
```
Subject: Wednesday Meeting Rescheduled to Friday 2 PM
```

Email 3:
```
Subject: Approve Vendor Contract by Friday EOD
```

**Why split:** Each topic requires a different action (none, calendar update, approval). Combined emails cause missed action items. Separate emails get separate responses and separate tracking.

## Mobile Optimization

60% of business emails are read on phones. Optimize for this reality.

**The mobile preview test:** Read only the subject line and first two sentences. If the reader cannot determine the email's purpose and required action from those elements alone, rewrite.

**Rules:**
1. Front-load critical information in the first two lines
2. Keep paragraphs to 2-4 sentences (long paragraphs become walls of text on small screens)
3. Use bullet points for any list of three or more items
4. Avoid tables wider than three columns (they break on mobile)
5. Subject line: 50 characters max for full visibility on mobile

## Constraint: One Topic Per Email

Every email covers exactly one topic. This is non-negotiable for business email.

**Why:** Multi-topic emails cause partial responses, missed action items, and broken email threads. When a recipient replies to one topic, the other topics get buried.

**Decision rule:** If you need to communicate about two topics, send two emails. If the topics are interdependent (e.g., "approve the budget so we can schedule the launch"), they are one topic.

## Common Failure Patterns

### Failure 1: Burying the Lead
The main point appears after two paragraphs of background. Fix by moving the request or key information to the first sentence.

### Failure 2: Vague Action Requests
"Please review when convenient" gives the reader no deadline and no specific action. Fix with: "Please approve by Friday, October 30."

### Failure 3: Missing Response Path
The email asks for something but does not tell the reader how to respond. Fix by ending with a specific, low-effort response instruction.

### Failure 4: Excessive Length
More than 300 words signals the content should be a memo, brief, or attached document. Fix by compressing to 150-300 words and linking to details.

### Failure 5: No Action Clarity
The reader finishes the email unsure whether a response is expected. Fix by explicitly stating "No action required" or "Please respond by [date]."

## Length Management

**Target:** 150-300 words (3-5 paragraphs)

**If exceeding 300 words, ask these questions in order:**
1. Am I covering more than one topic? Split into separate emails.
2. Can bullet points replace prose paragraphs?
3. Can I link to a document instead of explaining inline?
4. Should this be a memo or brief instead of an email?

**Compression priority (what to cut first):**
1. Background the reader already knows
2. Pleasantries and hedging language
3. Redundant restatements of the same point
4. Detail that belongs in an attachment

## Quality Checklist

Validate every email against these criteria before finalizing:

- [ ] Subject line is specific, action-oriented, and under 50 characters
- [ ] Main point appears in the first 1-2 sentences
- [ ] Required action is explicitly stated (or "no action required" is stated)
- [ ] Deadline is included when action is required
- [ ] Total length is 150-300 words across 3-5 paragraphs
- [ ] Tone matches the audience and relationship
- [ ] One topic per email (no multi-topic emails)
- [ ] Mobile preview test passes (first 2 lines stand alone)
- [ ] Closing tells the reader exactly how to respond
- [ ] Grammar and spelling are correct
