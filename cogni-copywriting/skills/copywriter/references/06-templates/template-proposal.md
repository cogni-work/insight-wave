# Business Proposal Template

<purpose>
This reference defines the canonical structure, section-level writing instructions, and quality criteria for generating business proposals. Use it as a blueprint: follow the section order, apply the annotated guidance within each section, and adapt scope (number of features, phases, etc.) to the complexity of the engagement.
</purpose>

<before-you-write>

## Pre-Generation Reasoning

Before drafting any proposal content, work through these decisions in order:

1. **Identify the framework** that best fits the engagement type:
   - **FAB (Feature-Advantage-Benefit):** The client is evaluating a product or service with distinct capabilities. Structure each solution component as Feature then Advantage then Benefit.
   - **PSB (Problem-Solution-Benefit):** The client has a clear pain point. Lead with the problem, present the solution, then quantify the benefit.
   - **Pyramid Principle:** The engagement requires a complex, multi-layered recommendation. State the conclusion first, then support it with grouped arguments.

2. **Determine proposal scope** from the engagement complexity:
   - Simple (single service, straightforward): 3-5 pages, 2-3 solution components, 2 phases
   - Standard (multiple components, moderate complexity): 5-10 pages, 3-5 solution components, 3 phases
   - Complex (enterprise, multi-phase, detailed): 10-20 pages, 5+ solution components, 4+ phases
   - RFP response: Follow RFP structure exactly; length as specified by the RFP

3. **Establish the client-centric lens.** Every sentence must answer the reader's implicit question: "What does this mean for me?" Use "you/your" language over "we/our" language. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

</before-you-write>

<template>

## Section 1: Title Block

```
# [Proposal Title]
**[Your Company Name] | Prepared for [Client Name] | [Date]**
```

<guidance>
- The title must be descriptive AND client-focused. Bad: "Digital Transformation Proposal." Good: "Accelerating [Client Name]'s Order Fulfillment Through Automated Workflow Integration."
- Include all three elements on the subtitle line: author company, recipient, and date.
</guidance>

---

## Section 2: Executive Summary

```
## Executive Summary

[3-5 sentence paragraph]

**Investment:** [Total cost or range]
**Timeline:** [Duration of engagement or delivery]
**Expected Outcome:** [Primary result with a quantified metric]
```

<guidance>
This is the single most important section. Many decision-makers read only this. It must stand completely alone.

Compose the paragraph using this four-part structure in exactly this order:
1. What you are proposing (one sentence, plain language)
2. Why it matters to the client right now (one sentence linking to their strategic priority or pain)
3. The key quantified benefit (one sentence with a specific metric, percentage, or dollar figure)
4. Investment and timeline summary (one sentence)

Quality checks:
- A reader who sees nothing else should understand the full proposal from this section alone.
- Must contain at least one concrete number (cost, percentage improvement, timeline).
- Must be readable in under 60 seconds.
- Avoid jargon; write for a non-technical executive audience.
</guidance>

---

## Section 3: Understanding Your Challenges

```
## Understanding Your Challenges

[Paragraph 1: Current state and primary challenge]

[Paragraph 2: Secondary challenges or compounding effects]

[Paragraph 3: The client's goals and definition of success]
```

<guidance>
This section builds trust by proving you understand the client's situation before proposing anything.

Write each paragraph with these specific goals:

**Paragraph 1 -- Primary challenge:**
- Describe the client's current situation and their central problem.
- Reference specifics from discovery conversations, industry context, or known pain points.
- State the measurable impact of the problem (cost, time, risk, missed revenue).

**Paragraph 2 -- Compounding effects:**
- Identify 2-3 secondary issues that make the primary challenge worse.
- Describe what happens if the problem is not addressed (status quo cost).
- Use concrete consequences, not vague warnings.

**Paragraph 3 -- Client goals:**
- Articulate what success looks like from the client's perspective.
- Connect their goals to strategic objectives (growth, efficiency, competitive positioning).
- Use their own language and terminology where possible.

Quality checks:
- Zero mentions of your company or solution in this section. This is entirely about the client.
- Every claim should be something the client would nod along to, not something they would dispute.
- Demonstrate insight, not just information. Show you understand the "why" behind the symptoms.
</guidance>

---

## Section 4: Our Solution

```
## Our Solution

[1-2 paragraph high-level overview of the approach and why it fits this client's situation]

### [Component Name 1]

**What we deliver:**
[2-3 sentences describing this component in plain language.]

**Why this approach:**
[2-3 sentences explaining the advantage over alternatives, specific to the client's context.]

**Value to you:**
- [Quantified benefit with metric]
- [Quantified benefit with metric]
- [Quantified benefit with metric]

### [Component Name 2]
[Same three-part structure: What / Why / Value]

### [Component Name 3]
[Same three-part structure: What / Why / Value]
```

<guidance>
This is the heart of the proposal. Structure depends on the chosen framework:

**If using FAB:** Each component follows the Feature-Advantage-Benefit pattern shown above. The "What we deliver" block is the Feature, "Why this approach" is the Advantage, and "Value to you" bullets are the Benefits.

**If using PSB:** Restructure each component as: restate the specific problem this component solves, describe the solution, then list the benefits.

**If using Pyramid:** Lead with the overall recommendation in the overview paragraph, then present each component as a supporting argument with evidence.

For each component:
- The "What we deliver" block uses plain, non-technical language. A non-specialist must understand it.
- The "Why this approach" block compares against the client's likely alternatives (doing nothing, competitor approaches, DIY). Be specific, not generic.
- Every "Value to you" bullet must contain a number: a percentage, a dollar figure, a time saved, or a quantity. Avoid vague claims like "improved efficiency." Write instead: "Reduce invoice processing time from 5 days to 1 day."

Adapt the number of components to the engagement scope (see pre-generation reasoning). Use 2-3 for simple proposals, 3-5 for standard, 5+ for complex.

Quality checks:
- Every component directly addresses a challenge from Section 3. No orphaned features.
- Benefits are written from the client's perspective ("You will see..."), not yours ("We will deliver...").
- At least one benefit per component includes a financial metric (revenue gain, cost reduction, or risk avoidance in dollar terms).
</guidance>

## Section 5: Implementation Approach

```
## Implementation Approach

### Phase 1: [Phase Name] ([Duration])
[2-3 sentences describing what happens in this phase.]
- [Key activity]
- [Key activity]
- [Key activity]

**Deliverable:** [What the client receives at the end of this phase]

### Phase 2: [Phase Name] ([Duration])
[Same structure]

### Phase 3: [Phase Name] ([Duration])
[Same structure]
```

<guidance>
This section proves you have thought through execution, not just strategy.

For each phase:
- Name the phase with an action-oriented label (e.g., "Discovery & Assessment" not "Phase 1").
- State the duration in specific terms (weeks, not "a few weeks").
- List 3-5 concrete activities, not vague descriptions.
- End each phase with a tangible deliverable the client can review, approve, or use.

Phase structure conventions:
- Phase 1 is almost always discovery, assessment, or planning.
- The final phase should include knowledge transfer, handoff, or success measurement.
- If the engagement has ongoing support, add a final "Ongoing Support" phase with monthly/quarterly cadence.

Quality checks:
- Phase durations must add up to the timeline stated in the Executive Summary.
- Every deliverable is something the client can hold, read, or measure -- not an abstract process.
- Activities use active verbs: "Conduct," "Analyze," "Build," "Test," "Deploy" -- never "Look into" or "Explore."
</guidance>

## Section 6: Investment & ROI

```
## Investment & ROI

### Investment Breakdown

| Component | Description | Investment |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| [Item 1]  | [Brief description] | [Cost] |
| [Item 2]  | [Brief description] | [Cost] |
| [Item 3]  | [Brief description] | [Cost] |
| **Total** |             | **[Total]** |

### Return on Investment

| Metric | Current State | Future State | Annual Impact |
|--------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| [Metric 1] | [Baseline] | [Projected] | [Savings/gain] |
| [Metric 2] | [Baseline] | [Projected] | [Savings/gain] |
| [Metric 3] | [Baseline] | [Projected] | [Savings/gain] |

**Payback Period:** [Time to recover investment]
**[3 or 5]-Year NPV:** [Net present value, if applicable]
```

<guidance>
Pricing must be transparent. Never hide costs or bury totals.

**Investment table:**
- Align line items with the phases or components described earlier. The reader should see a direct mapping.
- If pricing is a range, state the range and explain what drives the variance.
- Include payment terms or milestones if relevant (e.g., "50% at kickoff, 50% at delivery").

**ROI table:**
- Use metrics the client already tracks. Do not invent new KPIs.
- "Current State" and "Future State" must be grounded in evidence: benchmarks, discovery findings, or industry data.
- "Annual Impact" should be in dollars whenever possible, or in time saved converted to dollar equivalent.

**Payback period and NPV:**
- Include payback period for all proposals above $50K.
- Include NPV only for complex, multi-year engagements.
- If you lack data to calculate ROI precisely, state assumptions explicitly.

Quality checks:
- Total cost matches the "Investment" figure in the Executive Summary.
- ROI figures are conservative and defensible, not aspirational.
- The ROI section makes the investment feel small relative to the return.
</guidance>

## Section 7: Why [Your Company Name]

```
## Why [Company Name]

**Relevant Experience**
[2-3 sentences describing similar engagements with quantified outcomes. Name clients if permitted.]

**Unique Capabilities**
- [Credential, methodology, or differentiator]
- [Credential, methodology, or differentiator]
- [Credential, methodology, or differentiator]

**Risk Mitigation**
- [Guarantee, SLA, or safeguard]
- [Guarantee, SLA, or safeguard]
- [Guarantee, SLA, or safeguard]

**Your Team**
[Brief introduction of key personnel who will work on this engagement, with relevant experience for each.]
```

<guidance>
This section answers: "Why should we choose you over alternatives?"

**Relevant Experience:** Choose examples that mirror the client's industry, challenge type, or engagement size. Every example must include a quantified result ("reduced onboarding time by 40% for [Similar Client]").

**Unique Capabilities:** List only differentiators that matter for this specific engagement. Certifications, proprietary methodologies, team depth, or technology advantages. Do not list generic company facts.

**Risk Mitigation:** Proactively address the client's likely objections: timeline risk, budget overrun, quality concerns, or capability gaps. Use concrete mechanisms: SLAs with penalties, money-back guarantees, phased go/no-go gates, dedicated escalation paths.

**Your Team:** Name the actual people. Include their title, years of relevant experience, and one accomplishment relevant to this engagement. Do not use generic role descriptions.

Quality checks:
- Every point in this section is specific to this engagement, not boilerplate.
- The client should feel that choosing anyone else is risky after reading this section.
- Risk mitigation items directly counter the most likely objections for this type of engagement.
</guidance>

## Section 8: Next Steps

```
## Next Steps

1. **[Action]:** [Description with specific date or "within X days of proposal acceptance"]
2. **[Action]:** [Description with specific date]
3. **[Action]:** [Description with specific date]
4. **Project Kickoff:** [Proposed date, contingent on decision by specific date]

**Proposal Valid Until:** [Expiration date]
```

<guidance>
Create a clear path from "reading this proposal" to "project underway."

- Step 1 is always a low-commitment action: a call, a meeting, or signing a letter of intent.
- Steps should escalate in commitment gradually.
- Include specific dates or relative timeframes for every step.
- The "Proposal Valid Until" date creates urgency. Set it 2-4 weeks from the proposal date.

Quality checks:
- The first action is easy and low-risk for the client (e.g., "Schedule a 30-minute alignment call").
- Every step has a clear owner (implicit: the client, unless stated otherwise).
- The kickoff date is realistic given the decision timeline.
</guidance>

## Proposal Quality Standards

Apply these standards as a final validation pass before outputting any proposal.

### Language and Tone
- **Formality:** High. Professional, polished, confident but not arrogant.
- **Voice:** Active voice for 80%+ of sentences. "We will deliver" not "Will be delivered."
- **Perspective:** Client-centric. "You will achieve" over "We will deliver." Ratio target: 2:1 you-language to we-language.
- **Specificity:** Every benefit claim must include a number. Replace "improve efficiency" with "reduce processing time by 35%."
- **Readability:** Target Flesch reading ease score of 50-60. Standard difficulty appropriate for executive audiences.

### Structural Checks
- Executive Summary must function as a standalone document.
- Every solution component maps to a challenge described in Section 3.
- Phase durations sum to the timeline in the Executive Summary.
- Investment total matches the Executive Summary figure.
- ROI metrics use numbers the client already tracks.
- Next Steps begin with a low-commitment action.

### Formatting Standards
- Maximum 3 heading levels (##, ###, rarely ####).
- Paragraphs: 3-5 sentences each.
- Use horizontal rules (---) between major sections.
- Bold key terms, metrics, and financial figures.
- Use tables for all pricing, comparisons, and ROI data.
- Target approximately one visual element (table, list, or callout) per page.

### Common Failure Modes to Avoid
- Focusing on your company instead of client outcomes.
- Generic content that could apply to any client (missing personalization).
- Vague benefits without quantification.
- No clear differentiation from competitors.
- Buried, unclear, or missing pricing.
- No urgency or clear next steps.
- Technical jargon without plain language translation.
- Weak Executive Summary that does not stand alone.
- Solution components that do not trace back to stated challenges.

</quality-standards>
