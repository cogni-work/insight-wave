# Executive Impact

## Quick Reference

**Use when:** Writing for C-suite, board members, senior leadership, key stakeholders
**Core principle:** Executives decide; give them what they need in the format they expect
**Key techniques:** Lead with recommendation, quantify everything, respect time
**Target:** Decision-ready in 60 seconds of reading

## How to Think About Executive Content

Before writing any executive-facing content, reason through these questions in order:

1. **What decision or action do I need from this person?** If you cannot state it in one sentence, the content is not ready to write.
2. **What is the minimum information they need to make that decision?** Everything else is appendix material.
3. **What numbers make the case?** If there are no numbers, the case is not yet made.
4. **What is the cost of inaction?** Executives prioritize by consequence, not by importance claims.

This reasoning sequence determines the structure. Work through it before generating any content.

## Executive Reading Behavior

Executives do not read documents linearly. They scan for decision-relevant signals.

**Scanning pattern:** Headlines first, then bold text, then bullets, then selective deep-dive into sections that earned attention.

**Time budget:** 30-60 seconds for first pass. If the recommendation and rationale are not visible in that window, the document fails.

**Decision driver:** "What should I do?" always before "Why should I do it?"

**Trust filter:** Specificity and data signal credibility. Vague assertions signal incomplete analysis.

## The 5 Executive Imperatives

### Imperative 1: Lead with the Ask

Place the recommendation in the first two sentences. Rationale follows. Background comes last.

<before_after>
<before>
After extensive analysis of market conditions, competitive positioning, and internal capabilities, our team has developed several options for consideration regarding the cloud migration initiative. The following document outlines the methodology, findings, and recommendations from this six-month effort.
</before>
<after>
**Recommendation:** Approve $2.4M investment in AWS migration, starting Q3.

**Rationale:** 18-month payback, 340% three-year ROI, eliminates $800K annual maintenance on legacy systems. Delay past Q3 increases cost by $200K due to vendor contract timing.
</after>
<reasoning>
The before version buries the recommendation behind methodology description. An executive scanning this would get two sentences in and still not know what is being asked. The after version puts the decision in sentence one, the key numbers in sentence two, and the urgency driver in sentence three. An executive can approve, reject, or ask questions within 10 seconds.
</reasoning>
</before_after>

**Structure every executive document as:**
1. **Recommendation** -- what to do (1-2 sentences)
2. **Key rationale** -- why, with numbers (2-3 bullets)
3. **Supporting detail** -- for those who want depth (remainder of document)

### Imperative 2: Quantify Everything

Translate every benefit, risk, and outcome into a number. Unquantified claims read as opinions rather than analysis.

**Transformation table -- apply these conversions systematically:**

| Vague Claim (reject this) | Quantified Statement (use this) |
|---|---|
| "Improves efficiency" | "Reduces processing time from 12 days to 4.5 days" |
| "Saves money" | "Saves $400K annually ($33K/month)" |
| "Better customer experience" | "Increases NPS from 42 to 67 (+25 points)" |
| "Faster time to market" | "Launches 8 weeks earlier (May vs. July)" |
| "Reduces risk" | "Reduces compliance exposure from $3.1M to $800K" |
| "Strong adoption" | "87% adoption within 90 days (340 of 391 users)" |
| "Growing market" | "Market growing at 23% CAGR, reaching $4.2B by 2027" |

**Triple-quantify key benefits for maximum impact.** For each major claim, provide three number dimensions:

```
Investment: $400K annual savings (23% of current spend, 4:1 return on $100K investment)
Timeline: 8 weeks to deploy (3x faster than alternative, live before Q3 close)
Risk: $2.3M exposure eliminated (from 94th percentile to 12th percentile in audit risk)
```

**When exact numbers are unavailable,** use ranges with methodology notes rather than vague qualifiers:

<before_after>
<before>
This should significantly reduce costs.
</before>
<after>
Estimated savings of $300K-$450K annually, based on pilot results from the Northeast region (n=340 users, 6-month period). Conservative estimate uses lower bound.
</after>
<reasoning>
"Significantly" is meaningless to an executive making a resource allocation decision. The range with methodology gives them both the number and the confidence level. The conservative-estimate note shows analytical rigor.
</reasoning>
</before_after>

### Imperative 3: Respect Time Scarcity

Every unnecessary word is a signal that the author does not understand executive priorities. Ruthless brevity is a form of respect.

**Word budgets by section:**

| Section | Word Budget | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Recommendation | 20-40 words | One to two sentences maximum |
| Executive Summary | 100-150 words | Only if document exceeds one page |
| Key Rationale | 50-80 words | Three to five bullets with numbers |
| Background/Context | 50-100 words | Most executives skip this entirely |
| Analysis | 150-200 words | Bullets preferred over paragraphs |
| Next Steps | 30-60 words | Three to five items, one line each |
| **Total one-pager** | **400-600 words** | **Hard ceiling** |

**Scanability rules:**
- Bold every key number and every decision point
- Use bullet lists instead of paragraphs for any list of three or more items
- Front-load value: the first sentence of every paragraph must be the key point of that paragraph
- If removing a sentence does not change the decision, remove it

<before_after>
<before>
As we continue to evaluate the various options available to us in terms of our digital transformation journey, it has become increasingly clear that the current approach, while having served us well in the past, is no longer sufficient to meet the growing demands of our customer base and the competitive pressures we face in the market today.
</before>
<after>
**Current platform cannot scale past 10K concurrent users.** We hit this ceiling twice in Q4, losing an estimated $340K in revenue. Three options follow, with our recommendation.
</after>
<reasoning>
The before version is 64 words that say "things need to change." The after version is 31 words that give the specific constraint, the evidence, and the cost, then signal what comes next. An executive reading the after version knows the problem, its impact, and that options are coming -- in three seconds.
</reasoning>
</before_after>

### Imperative 4: Provide Decision Clarity

Remove all ambiguity about what the executive needs to do, by when, and what happens if they do not.

**Four components of decision clarity:**

**1. The Ask -- make it binary or enumerated:**
- Reject: "Consider this proposal"
- Accept: "Approve Phase 1 funding of $500K"
- Accept: "Select Option A or Option B by March 15"

**2. The Options -- when choices exist, use a comparison structure:**

```
Option A (Recommended): Full migration to AWS
  Cost: $2.4M over 18 months | Return: $8.2M over 3 years | Risk: Medium (vendor lock-in)

Option B: Hybrid approach, migrate 60% of workloads
  Cost: $1.6M over 24 months | Return: $4.1M over 3 years | Risk: High (split architecture complexity)

Option C: Status quo
  Cost: $800K/year maintenance (increasing ~15% annually) | Return: None | Risk: High (EOL in 2028)
```

Always include a status quo option with its costs. Inaction is never free.

**3. The Timeline -- anchor to business consequences:**
- Reject: "Please respond at your convenience"
- Accept: "Decision needed by March 15 to meet Q2 implementation window"

**4. The Cost of Delay -- make inaction expensive:**
- Reject: "This is important"
- Accept: "Each month of delay costs $45K in manual processing and increases migration risk as vendor support narrows"

### Imperative 5: Signal Credibility

Executives are expert at detecting weak analysis. Every element of the document must signal thorough, honest work.

**Credibility builders:**

| Technique | Weak (triggers skepticism) | Strong (builds trust) |
|---|---|---|
| Precision | "about $130K" | "$127,400" |
| Attribution | "Research shows" | "Per Gartner's 2025 Magic Quadrant" |
| Methodology | "Testing indicates" | "Based on 6-month pilot across 3 regions (n=340)" |
| Risk honesty | (risks omitted) | "Key risk: single-vendor dependency. Mitigation: contractual exit clause and parallel capability build." |
| Comparison | "Strong performance" | "23% above industry median (Deloitte benchmark, 2025)" |

**Credibility destroyers -- avoid these patterns:**
- Vague claims without supporting data
- Omitting risks or challenges entirely (reads as either naivety or deception)
- Unrealistic projections without sensitivity analysis
- Unexplained jargon or acronyms (executives span multiple domains and will not decode your shorthand)
- Hyperbolic language: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "unprecedented" (reads as marketing, not analysis)
- Buzzword accumulation: "synergize," "leverage," "ideate" (signals that substance is thin)

## Executive Document Templates

Use these as structural scaffolds. Adapt section lengths to content, but preserve the information order.

### Board Memo

```markdown
**TO:** [Board / Executive Committee]
**FROM:** [Name, Title]
**DATE:** [Date]
**RE:** [Action Required: Specific Topic]

## Recommendation
[One sentence: what you want them to do, including the dollar amount or key metric]

## Strategic Context
[Two to three sentences: why this matters now, tied to a business objective or external pressure]

## Key Financials
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Investment Required | $X |
| Expected Return | $Y over Z years |
| Payback Period | N months |
| Risk Exposure (if no action) | $W |

## Options Considered
1. **Recommended:** [Option] -- [One-line rationale with key number]
2. **Alternative:** [Option] -- [Why not chosen, in one line]
3. **Status Quo:** [Cost and risk of inaction]

## Next Steps (if approved)
1. [Action] -- [Owner] -- [Date]
2. [Action] -- [Owner] -- [Date]
3. [Action] -- [Owner] -- [Date]

## Appendix
[Supporting data, methodology notes, detailed analysis for deep-dive]
```

### Executive Summary

```markdown
## Executive Summary

**Bottom Line:** [One sentence: the recommendation or key finding, with the headline number]

**Key Findings:**
- [Finding with quantification and source]
- [Finding with quantification and source]
- [Finding with quantification and source]

**Recommended Action:** [Specific ask -- what, how much, by when]

**Investment:** $X | **Return:** $Y | **Timeline:** Z months | **Risk of Delay:** [consequence]
```

### Decision Request

```markdown
## Decision Required: [Specific Topic]

**Request:** [Exact approval or decision needed, in one sentence]
**Deadline:** [Date] -- [Business reason for deadline]

**Context:** [Two to three sentences: only the background needed to decide]

**Recommendation:** Option A -- [one-line rationale with key number]

| | Option A (Recommended) | Option B | Status Quo |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | $X | $Y | $Z/year (growing) |
| Return | $A over B years | $C over D years | None |
| Risk | [Primary risk] | [Primary risk] | [Primary risk] |
| Timeline | N months | M months | N/A |

**Risk of Delay:** [Quantified consequence per week or month of inaction]
```

## Impact Techniques for Executive Context

### Metrics Framing

Always present key metrics in a multi-dimensional format. A single number lacks context; three dimensions tell a story.

```
ROI: 340% over 3 years
Payback: 18 months
vs. Industry Average: 2.3x (source: Forrester 2025)
vs. Last Year: 45% improvement
```

Apply this pattern to the two or three most important metrics in every executive document.

### Word Choice

**High-credibility vocabulary for executives:**
Strategic, proven, validated, risk-adjusted, scalable, sustainable, measurable, accelerated, competitive, margin-positive

**Low-credibility vocabulary to avoid:**
Revolutionary, game-changing, exciting, unprecedented, synergy, leverage, ideate, paradigm-shifting, best-in-class (without data), world-class (without benchmark)

**The test:** If a word could describe any initiative at any company, it adds no information. Replace it with a specific claim.

<before_after>
<before>
This game-changing solution will revolutionize our approach to customer engagement and deliver unprecedented results.
</before>
<after>
This platform reduces customer response time from 4.2 hours to 12 minutes and is projected to increase retention by 8 points (from 71% to 79%), based on the Phase 1 pilot.
</after>
<reasoning>
The before version uses four hyperbolic adjectives and makes zero specific claims. An executive cannot distinguish this from any other pitch. The after version makes two specific, verifiable claims with a methodology note. The executive can now evaluate whether those specific outcomes justify the investment.
</reasoning>
</before_after>

### Rhetorical Devices

Use sparingly and only in high-stakes closing statements. Executives respond to clarity, not style -- but a well-placed device can make a recommendation memorable.

**Rule of Three** for summarizing key points:
"Three priorities: reduce cost, accelerate delivery, maintain quality."

**Antithesis** for framing the decision:
"The question is not whether we can afford this investment -- it is whether we can afford the cost of inaction."

**Short declarative close** for emphasis after detailed analysis:
"The data supports this. The timing is right. We recommend approval."

Limit rhetorical devices to the recommendation or closing section. Using them in the analysis section undermines the data-driven tone.

## Pre-Send Validation Checklist

Before finalizing any executive-facing content, verify each item. A single "no" means the document is not ready.

| # | Check | What to Fix If Failed |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Recommendation appears in first two sentences | Move it; push background to later section |
| 2 | Every benefit has a number attached | Quantify or remove the claim |
| 3 | Specific decision or action is stated explicitly | Add "Request: [exact ask]" |
| 4 | Deadline is stated with business rationale | Add date and consequence of missing it |
| 5 | Fits on one page (or has a standalone executive summary) | Cut background; move detail to appendix |
| 6 | Scannable in 30 seconds (bold, bullets, headers) | Reformat; break paragraphs into bullets |
| 7 | At least one risk is acknowledged with mitigation | Add "Key risk: X. Mitigation: Y." |
| 8 | Data sources are cited (not "research shows") | Add source name, date, and sample size |
| 9 | No unexplained jargon or acronyms | Spell out or replace with plain language |
| 10 | Every paragraph passes the "so what?" test | If removing it does not change the decision, remove it |

## Common Failures and Fixes

| Failure Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Burying the recommendation | Executive stops reading before reaching the ask | First two sentences must contain the recommendation |
| Unquantified benefits | No basis for evaluating the decision | Attach a number to every claim |
| Missing ask | Executive does not know what action is needed | State "Request: [specific action]" explicitly |
| Exceeding one page | Document will not be read completely | Cut to one page; overflow goes to appendix |
| No deadline | Request gets deprioritized indefinitely | Add deadline with cost-of-delay consequence |
| Risk-free presentation | Signals incomplete analysis or dishonesty | Acknowledge top one to two risks with mitigations |
| Hyperbolic language | Triggers executive skepticism filter | Replace every adjective with a specific metric |
