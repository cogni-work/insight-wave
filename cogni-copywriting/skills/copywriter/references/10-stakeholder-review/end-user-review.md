# End-User Review Criteria

## Quick Reference

- **Use when:** Document targets general audiences, customers, non-specialists, or end-users of a product/service
- **Core principle:** If the average reader cannot understand it in one pass, it fails
- **Evaluation focus:** Plain language, immediate clarity, actionability, visual scannability, empathetic tone

## Perspective Philosophy

You are evaluating this document as if you are a non-specialist reader who:
- Has no insider knowledge of the organization, product, or domain
- Will scan the document quickly and move on if confused
- Needs to know what this means for them and what to do next
- Will disengage if the tone feels condescending, bureaucratic, or threatening

Your job is to catch every barrier between the document and the reader's understanding.

**Flag these anti-patterns immediately:**
- Jargon or acronyms used without definition
- Dense paragraphs that require slow, careful reading
- Missing, vague, or implied action steps
- Condescending or bureaucratic tone
- Assumptions that the reader has insider context

---

## Evaluation Process

Work through each of the five criteria below in order. For each criterion:

1. Read the evaluation question
2. Examine the document against the PASS, CONCERN, and FAIL indicators
3. Assign exactly one rating (PASS, CONCERN, or FAIL)
4. Note specific evidence from the document supporting your rating
5. Draft concrete recommendations for any non-PASS rating

---

### Criterion 1: Plain Language (Weight: 30%)

**Evaluation question:** Is the document written in simple, everyday language that a general reader can understand without specialized knowledge?

**How to evaluate — check each of these in sequence:**

1. Scan for jargon, acronyms, and technical terms. Are all of them defined on first use?
2. Check sentence length. Is the average under 20 words?
3. Check word choice. Are simple alternatives used (e.g., "use" not "utilize", "help" not "facilitate", "start" not "initiate")?
4. Check voice. Is active voice dominant (e.g., "We recommend" not "It is recommended")?
5. Check for insider assumptions. Would a reader with zero context understand every sentence?

**PASS (score: 100):**
Plain language throughout. Every technical term is defined on first use. Short, common words preferred. Active voice dominant. Average sentence length under 20 words.

Example of PASS:
> "Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system -- a tool that helps track every customer interaction in one place."

**CONCERN (score: 60):**
Mostly plain language, but some undefined jargon or unnecessarily complex phrasing remains. One or two sentences require re-reading.

Example of CONCERN:
> "The CRM system integrates with your tech stack." ("tech stack" is undefined jargon)

**FAIL (score: 0):**
Heavy jargon, undefined acronyms, or complex terminology that blocks comprehension for a general reader.

Example of FAIL:
> "Leverage synergistic paradigms to optimize downstream workflows."

**Common word substitutions to check for:**

| Flag this | Replace with |
|-----------|-------------|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help |
| implement | set up, start |
| leverage | use |
| optimize | improve |
| stakeholder | people involved |
| paradigm | approach, method |
| synergy | working together |
| interface (verb) | connect, work with |
| bandwidth (non-technical) | time, capacity |

### Criterion 2: Immediate Clarity (Weight: 25%)

**Evaluation question:** Can a general reader grasp the main point within the first paragraph?

**How to evaluate — apply the 3-question test:**

After reading only the first paragraph, can the reader answer:
1. What is this about?
2. Why does it matter to me?
3. What (if anything) do I need to do?

If the answer to any of these is "no" or "unclear," the document has a clarity problem.

**PASS (score: 100):**
The main point is crystal clear in the first 1-2 sentences. The reader immediately knows what this is about, why it matters to them, and what action (if any) is needed. Key information is visually emphasized.

Example of PASS:
> "**We are switching to a new customer tracking system on March 1.** This means faster responses to your questions and better service. Here is what you need to do to prepare."

**CONCERN (score: 60):**
The main point is stated somewhere in the first paragraph but is diluted by context, background, or hedging. The reader has to work to extract the key message.

Example of CONCERN:
> "As part of our ongoing commitment to improving service quality, we have been evaluating several options for enhancing our customer management capabilities. We are pleased to announce some changes coming soon."

(The reader learns "changes coming soon" but not what, when, or why it matters to them.)

**FAIL (score: 0):**
The main point is buried after multiple paragraphs of background, context, or history. The reader must read the entire document to understand what it is about.

Example of FAIL:
> Paragraph 1: company history. Paragraph 2: market trends. Paragraph 3: evaluation process. Paragraph 4: finally mentions the actual change.

**Edge case:** If a document is purely informational with no action required, PASS still requires the "what" and "why it matters" to be clear in the first paragraph. The answer to "What do I need to do?" can legitimately be "Nothing -- this is for your awareness."

### Criterion 3: Actionability (Weight: 20%)

**Evaluation question:** Are next steps crystal clear with specific, concrete instructions?

**How to evaluate — check each action statement for four elements:**

1. **Specific verb:** Does it use a concrete action word? ("Review," "Click," "Call" -- not "Consider," "Familiarize," or "Explore")
2. **Deadline:** Is there a specific date? ("by February 28" -- not "soon," "at your earliest convenience," or "in the coming weeks")
3. **Method:** Does it explain how? ("Click the Confirm button in the email we sent" -- not "update your preferences")
4. **Support contact:** Is there a way to get help? (Name, email, phone number -- not just "contact us")

**PASS (score: 100):**
Step-by-step instructions with no ambiguity. Every action includes a specific verb, deadline, method, and support contact. Required vs. optional actions are clearly distinguished.

Example of PASS:
> **What you need to do:**
> 1. Review your account settings by February 28
> 2. Click "Confirm" in the email we sent on February 15
> 3. Call 1-800-555-0100 if you have questions (Mon-Fri, 9am-5pm)

**CONCERN (score: 60):**
Actions are stated but missing one or more of the four elements above.

Examples of CONCERN:
> "Please update your preferences." (Missing: how? by when?)
> "Contact us if you have questions." (Missing: how to contact?)
> "Review the new guidelines before the deadline." (Missing: which deadline? where are the guidelines?)

**FAIL (score: 0):**
No clear action steps, or the expected action is only implied.

Examples of FAIL:
> "We encourage you to familiarize yourself with the new system." (How? By when? What counts as "familiarized"?)
> Document ends without telling the reader what to do next.

**Edge case:** Some documents are purely informational with no action required. In that case, explicitly stating "No action is needed on your part" counts as PASS for actionability. Leaving the reader to guess whether they need to do something counts as CONCERN.

### Criterion 4: Visual Scannability (Weight: 15%)

**Evaluation question:** Is the document structured so a reader can scan it in under 60 seconds and find the key information?

**How to evaluate — check these structural elements:**

1. **Headers:** Are there clear H2/H3 headers breaking up content every 3-4 paragraphs?
2. **Paragraph length:** Are paragraphs 4 lines or fewer?
3. **Lists:** Are sets of 3+ items in bullet or numbered lists (not embedded in prose)?
4. **Emphasis:** Are key dates, numbers, and actions bolded or otherwise visually distinct?
5. **White space:** Is there breathing room between sections?

**PASS (score: 100):**
Excellent scan structure. Clear header hierarchy. Short paragraphs (4 lines max). Bullet lists for multiple items. Key information bolded. Adequate white space between sections.

**CONCERN (score: 60):**
Some structure present but with dense sections. Examples: headers used but some paragraphs exceed 5 lines; lists of items embedded in prose instead of bulleted; key dates or numbers not visually emphasized.

**FAIL (score: 0):**
Wall of text. Long paragraphs (7+ lines) with no visual breaks. No or minimal headers. No bullets. Key information indistinguishable from surrounding text.

**Quick structural test:** If you blur your eyes and look at the document shape, can you see distinct sections with varying visual density (headers, bullets, short paragraphs, white space)? If it looks like one uniform block, it fails.

### Criterion 5: Empathy and Tone (Weight: 10%)

**Evaluation question:** Is the tone respectful, helpful, and appropriate for a general audience?

**How to evaluate — check for these tone markers:**

Positive indicators (look for these):
- "You" and "your" language (addressing the reader directly)
- Helpful framing: "Here is how," "Here is what this means for you"
- Acknowledgment of impact: "We know change can be disruptive"
- Conversational but professional register

Negative indicators (flag these):
- Impersonal language: "Users must," "Customers are required to"
- Condescension: "As any informed user would know," "Obviously"
- Threatening tone: "Failure to comply will result in," "Non-compliance penalties"
- Bureaucratic distance: "It has been determined that," "Users are hereby notified"

**PASS (score: 100):**
Warm, helpful, direct tone. The reader feels respected and supported. The document speaks to them, not at them.

Example of PASS:
> "Here is what is changing and what it means for you. We know changes can be confusing -- we are here to help."

**CONCERN (score: 60):**
Professional but impersonal, or occasionally lapsing into bureaucratic or demanding language.

Example of CONCERN:
> "Users must familiarize themselves with new procedures prior to the transition date."

**FAIL (score: 0):**
Condescending, threatening, dismissive, or so formal that it creates emotional distance from the reader.

Examples of FAIL:
> "As any informed user would know, these changes are standard practice."
> "Failure to update your account by the deadline will result in suspension of services."

**Edge case:** Legal or compliance documents may require formal language. In that case, evaluate whether the document provides a plain-language summary alongside the formal text. If it does, score the plain-language portion. If it presents only legalese with no accessible alternative, flag it as CONCERN (not FAIL, since the formality may be required).

## Scoring

### Calculation

1. Rate each criterion: PASS = 100, CONCERN = 60, FAIL = 0
2. Apply weights:

| Criterion | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Plain Language | 0.30 |
| Immediate Clarity | 0.25 |
| Actionability | 0.20 |
| Visual Scannability | 0.15 |
| Empathy and Tone | 0.10 |

3. Multiply each score by its weight and sum for a total between 0 and 100.

### Thresholds

| Range | Rating | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 85-100 | Excellent | Highly accessible, ready for general audience |
| 70-84 | Good | Minor clarity gaps, small revisions needed |
| 50-69 | Concerns | Accessibility issues, significant revisions needed |
| 0-49 | Failing | Not user-friendly, requires major rewrite |

### Worked Example

- Plain Language: PASS (100 x 0.30 = 30)
- Immediate Clarity: CONCERN (60 x 0.25 = 15)
- Actionability: PASS (100 x 0.20 = 20)
- Visual Scannability: CONCERN (60 x 0.15 = 9)
- Empathy and Tone: PASS (100 x 0.10 = 10)
- **Total: 84** (Good)

## Output Format

Return your review as structured JSON:

```json
{
  "perspective": "end-user",
  "score": 84,
  "criteria_scores": {
    "plain_language": 100,
    "immediate_clarity": 60,
    "actionability": 100,
    "visual_scannability": 60,
    "empathy_tone": 100
  },
  "strengths": [
    "Specific evidence from document supporting each strength"
  ],
  "concerns": [
    "Specific evidence from document supporting each concern"
  ],
  "recommendations": [
    "CRITICAL: [Recommendation for issues that block comprehension]",
    "HIGH: [Recommendation for issues that reduce clarity]",
    "OPTIONAL: [Recommendation for nice-to-have improvements]"
  ]
}
```

**Requirements for each field:**
- **strengths:** 2-4 items. Each must cite specific text or structural elements from the document. Do not use generic praise.
- **concerns:** 1-4 items. Each must cite the specific passage, section, or structural problem. Do not use vague descriptions.
- **recommendations:** 2-5 items. Each must be a specific, implementable action. Prefix with priority level (CRITICAL, HIGH, or OPTIONAL). CRITICAL = blocks reader comprehension. HIGH = reduces clarity or usability. OPTIONAL = improves polish.

## Common Improvement Patterns

When a criterion scores CONCERN or FAIL, apply the relevant pattern below in your recommendations.

### Pattern 1: Simplify Language
**Before:** "Utilize the integrated CRM solution to facilitate customer engagement optimization."
**After:** "Use the new customer system to respond to customers faster."
**What changed:** Replaced jargon (CRM, facilitate, optimization) with plain equivalents. Shortened sentence. Made the benefit concrete.

### Pattern 2: Front-Load the Main Point
**Before:** Paragraph 1: background. Paragraph 2: context. Paragraph 3: the actual point.
**After:** First sentence: **"We are switching to a new customer system on March 1."**
**What changed:** Moved the key message from paragraph 3 to sentence 1. Background moved to a later section for readers who want context.

### Pattern 3: Make Actions Specific
**Before:** "Please familiarize yourself with the new system at your earliest convenience."
**After:**
> **What you need to do:**
> 1. Watch the 3-minute tutorial at [link] by February 28
> 2. Log in to test your access
> 3. Call 1-800-555-0100 if you need help

**What changed:** Replaced vague directive with numbered steps. Added specific method, deadline, and support contact.

### Pattern 4: Break Up Dense Text
**Before:** An 8-line paragraph listing benefits inline with commas.
**After:**
> **Key benefits:**
> - Faster responses (24 hours down to 4 hours)
> - Complete history in one place
> - Mobile access anywhere

**What changed:** Extracted inline list into bullets. Added specific metric for the first benefit. Reduced cognitive load.

### Pattern 5: Warm Up Cold Tone
**Before:** "Users are hereby notified that account migration is mandatory. Non-compliance will result in service interruption."
**After:** "Your account is moving to a new system on March 1. We will handle most of the work -- here is the one thing we need you to do."
**What changed:** Replaced impersonal "users" with "your." Removed threatening language. Framed the action as simple and supported.

## Conflict Resolution

When end-user perspective conflicts with other stakeholder perspectives:

| Conflict | Resolution |
|----------|------------|
| **Executive wants concise, End-user wants detailed** | Executive summary at top + detailed FAQ section below |
| **Technical wants precision, End-user wants simplicity** | Plain language in body + technical glossary as appendix |
| **Legal wants disclaimers, End-user wants clarity** | Main message in plain language + legal disclaimer in separate clearly-labeled section |
| **Marketing wants sophistication, End-user wants simple** | Simple language with aspirational framing (simple words, ambitious meaning) |

**Priority rule:** If the document audience is explicitly "general public," "customers," or "end-users," the end-user perspective takes priority over all other stakeholder perspectives in cases of unresolvable conflict.
