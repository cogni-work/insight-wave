# Marketing Review Criteria

You are evaluating a document from the marketing stakeholder perspective. Your goal is to determine whether the document will persuade its intended audience to take action. Think like a senior marketing strategist reviewing copy before it ships.

## Quick Reference

- **Use when:** Document aims to influence, persuade, promote, or engage audiences
- **Core principle:** Persuasion requires audience resonance -- speak to their motivations, not yours
- **Evaluation focus:** Audience resonance, persuasive structure, brand tone, call-to-action, emotional connection

## How to Use This Reference

Follow these steps in order:

1. Read the document under review in full before scoring anything.
2. For each of the 5 criteria below, think step by step:
   - Identify specific passages that are relevant to the criterion.
   - Compare those passages against the PASS, CONCERN, and FAIL descriptions.
   - Assign a score (PASS = 100, CONCERN = 60, FAIL = 0).
   - Record the specific passages that justify your score.
3. Calculate the weighted total.
4. Generate structured feedback using the output template at the end.

## Perspective Philosophy

Marketing stakeholders evaluate whether a document will actually move an audience. The central question is: **"Would the target audience read this and feel compelled to act?"**

A document passes marketing review when it demonstrates all five of these qualities:

- **Audience-centric language** -- Speaks to what the reader wants, fears, or aspires to, not what the organization wants to say
- **Persuasive structure** -- Leads with outcomes and benefits, supports with features and process
- **Brand consistency** -- Tone matches the organizational voice without jarring shifts
- **Clear call-to-action** -- The reader knows exactly what to do next, when, and why it matters
- **Emotional connection** -- Goes beyond pure logic to tap into values, identity, or aspiration

### Anti-Patterns to Flag

When you encounter any of the following, note them as concerns or failures depending on severity:

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Inside-out thinking | "We are proud to offer..." | Audience does not care about your pride |
| Feature dumping | Lists of technical capabilities with no benefit translation | Features do not persuade; outcomes do |
| Corporate-speak | "Leverage synergies to optimize..." | Generic language signals lazy thinking |
| Missing CTA | Ends with "Thank you for your consideration" | Without a next step, inaction wins |
| All logic, no emotion | Reads like a technical specification | Decisions are emotional first, rationalized second |

## Evaluation Checklist

### 1. Audience Resonance (Weight: 30%)

**Evaluation question:** Does the document speak to audience motivations, pain points, and aspirations rather than organizational priorities?

Think through these sub-questions before scoring:
- Who is the target audience, and what do they care about?
- Does the language address the reader directly ("you") or talk about the organization ("we")?
- Are audience pain points named explicitly?
- Are benefits framed in terms of what the reader gains?

**Scoring:**

**PASS (100):** Language consistently addresses the audience perspective. "You" focus dominates. Pain points are named. Benefits are specific and reader-relevant.

Example of PASS-level writing:
> "You'll reduce customer churn by 15%, freeing your team to focus on growth initiatives instead of firefighting retention issues."

This passes because it: names a specific benefit (15% churn reduction), addresses the reader directly ("your team"), and connects to an aspiration (growth over firefighting).

Quantitative check: "You/your" vs. "we/our" ratio of 3:1 or higher.

**CONCERN (60):** Some audience focus but frequent inside-out language. Benefits are present but vague or intermixed with organization-focused statements.

Example of CONCERN-level writing:
> "Our CRM system has advanced features that improve efficiency."

This is a concern because: "our" leads, "advanced features" is vague, and "improve efficiency" lacks specificity about what the reader actually gains.

Quantitative check: "You/your" vs. "we/our" ratio around 1:1.

**FAIL (0):** Primarily organization-focused language. The reader would have to infer how this applies to them.

Example of FAIL-level writing:
> "We are proud to offer a comprehensive solution that leverages cutting-edge technology to deliver enterprise-grade capabilities."

This fails because: every clause is about the organization, there is no reader benefit, and the language is entirely generic.

**Transformation pattern for recommendations:**

| Before (Inside-Out) | After (Audience-Centric) | What Changed |
|---|---|---|
| "We offer a comprehensive CRM platform" | "You get a complete view of every customer interaction in one dashboard" | Subject flipped from organization to reader; abstract capability replaced with concrete outcome |
| "Our team has 20 years of experience" | "You work with specialists who have solved this exact problem 200+ times" | Credential reframed as reader benefit; specificity added |

---

### 2. Persuasive Structure (Weight: 25%)

**Evaluation question:** Does the document follow a benefits-first structure, leading with outcomes before explaining features or process?

Think through these sub-questions before scoring:
- What does the reader encounter first: a benefit, a feature, or background context?
- Are features always connected to a benefit, or do some stand alone?
- Would the reader understand the value proposition from the first two paragraphs alone?

**Scoring:**

**PASS (100):** Benefits-driven structure throughout. The value proposition is clear within the first two paragraphs. Features always appear alongside or after their corresponding benefits.

Example of PASS-level structure:
> "**Reduce churn 15% and free 20 hours/week** by automating follow-ups and centralizing customer data in a single dashboard."

This passes because: the outcome leads (churn reduction, time saved), the mechanism follows (automation, centralization), and both are specific.

Structural check: The document follows one of these persuasive frameworks:
- **Outcome -> Benefit -> Advantage -> Feature** (strongest)
- **Problem -> Solution -> Benefit** (PSB)
- **Feature -> Advantage -> Benefit** (FAB -- acceptable if benefits are prominent)

**CONCERN (60):** Benefits are present but the document has feature-heavy sections where the reader must infer the value. Some passages lead with mechanism before establishing why it matters.

Example of CONCERN-level structure:
> Section opens with the benefit but then spends three paragraphs on system architecture before returning to outcomes. The reader loses the thread.

**FAIL (0):** Feature-first or process-first structure. Benefits are buried or absent. The reader would need to translate capabilities into value themselves.

Example of FAIL-level structure:
> "Our system uses PostgreSQL 14 with Redis caching and Kubernetes deployment for horizontal scaling."

This fails because: it is pure feature description with zero benefit translation. No audience member will care about the database engine unless they understand what it gives them.

**Persuasive hierarchy to look for (strongest to weakest):**

1. **Outcome** -- What success looks like for the reader
2. **Benefit** -- What the reader gains
3. **Advantage** -- Why this approach works better than alternatives
4. **Feature** -- What the solution includes or does

Strong documents spend most of their word count at levels 1-2 and use levels 3-4 as supporting evidence.

---

### 3. Brand Tone (Weight: 20%)

**Evaluation question:** Is the tone internally consistent throughout the document, and does it match what you would expect for the stated audience and context?

Think through these sub-questions before scoring:
- Is the tone consistent from start to finish, or does it shift?
- Does the vocabulary level match the audience?
- Is the degree of formality appropriate for the context?

**Scoring:**

**PASS (100):** Tone is consistent throughout and appropriate for the audience. No jarring shifts between sections.

The tone should match one of these archetypes (or a deliberate blend):

| Tone | Characterized By | Example Phrasing |
|---|---|---|
| Professional | Clear, concise, credible, jargon-free | "Based on our analysis...", "The data indicates..." |
| Approachable | Conversational but not casual, empathetic | "Here's what this means for you", "Let's break this down" |
| Innovative | Forward-looking, aspirational, confident | "Reimagine how your team...", "The next generation of..." |
| Authoritative | Definitive, data-driven, evidence-based | "Proven to deliver...", "Validated across 500+ deployments" |

**CONCERN (60):** Tone is mostly aligned but has noticeable inconsistencies. Common symptoms: shifts from professional to overly casual mid-document, or brand positioning is innovative but language is conservative and generic.

Example: A startup positioning itself as innovative uses phrases like "pursuant to" and "it is recommended that" -- this mismatch signals a tone problem.

**FAIL (0):** Tone is significantly misaligned with the intended audience or internally inconsistent. Common symptoms: corporate jargon in material targeting end consumers, overly casual language in enterprise B2B context, or tone shifts multiple times within the document.

**Key diagnostic:** Read the first paragraph and the last paragraph back to back. If they feel like they were written by different people for different audiences, the document has a tone consistency problem.

---

### 4. Call-to-Action (Weight: 15%)

**Evaluation question:** Does the document contain a clear, specific, and compelling call-to-action that tells the reader exactly what to do next?

Think through these sub-questions before scoring:
- Is there an explicit next step for the reader?
- Is there a deadline or reason for urgency?
- Is there a reason to act (benefit of acting, cost of not acting)?
- Does the CTA appear early enough to be seen by skim-readers, and is it reinforced at the end?

**Scoring:**

**PASS (100):** Explicit CTA with a specific action, a deadline, and a compelling reason. CTA appears in at least two locations (executive summary/opening and conclusion).

Example of PASS-level CTA:
> "**Approve by March 15** to launch before Q2 peak season and capture the $200K revenue opportunity."

This passes because it contains all four CTA components:
- **Action verb:** Approve
- **Specificity:** What exactly to do (approve this proposal)
- **Urgency:** When and why now (March 15, before Q2 peak)
- **Benefit:** What happens when they act ($200K revenue opportunity)

**CONCERN (60):** CTA is present but vague, generic, or weak. Missing one or more of the four components.

Examples of CONCERN-level CTAs:
- "Please review and provide feedback" -- no urgency, unclear specific action
- "Approve by end of quarter" -- vague timeline, no benefit stated
- "Let us know your thoughts" -- passive, no specific action

**FAIL (0):** No CTA, or it is buried so deep in the conclusion that skim-readers will miss it entirely.

Examples of FAIL-level endings:
- "Thank you for your consideration" -- this is a sign-off, not a CTA
- "We look forward to hearing from you" -- passive, no direction
- Document simply ends after the last content section with no next step

**Why this matters:** Without a clear CTA, the default action is no action. Every persuasive document must answer: "What do you want me to do, by when, and why should I do it now?"

---

### 5. Emotional Connection (Weight: 10%)

**Evaluation question:** Does the document go beyond pure logic to connect with the audience's values, identity, or aspirations?

Think through these sub-questions before scoring:
- Does the document create any relatable scenario or picture of success?
- Are there appeals to values the audience cares about (growth, security, innovation, control)?
- Is there a balance between rational and emotional appeals, or is it purely transactional?

**Scoring:**

**PASS (100):** Emotional appeals are present and balanced with rational arguments. The document paints a picture of success or connects to audience values at least once.

Example of PASS-level emotional connection:
> "Imagine your team spending time building relationships instead of chasing down lost customer data -- that's the freedom this system delivers."

This passes because it: creates a relatable before/after scenario, connects to an emotional value (freedom), and grounds the emotion in a concrete benefit.

Emotional triggers to look for (used appropriately for the audience):

| Trigger | Signal Words | Appropriate For |
|---|---|---|
| Aspiration | Transform, achieve, lead, pioneer | Growth-oriented audiences |
| Security | Protect, safeguard, proven, reliable | Risk-averse audiences |
| Control | Empower, enable, freedom, autonomy | Audiences frustrated by current constraints |
| Belonging | Join, partner, collaborate | Community-oriented audiences |
| Innovation | Next-generation, cutting-edge, future-proof | Early adopters, technology leaders |

**CONCERN (60):** Document is primarily rational/transactional with minimal emotional language. It may state benefits but does not connect those benefits to how the reader will feel or what they value.

Example: "This will improve efficiency by 20%" is rational and valid, but it lacks emotional resonance. Compare: "Your team gets 20% of their week back -- time they can spend on the creative work that attracted them to this role in the first place."

**FAIL (0):** Purely rational, purely transactional, reads like a technical specification or financial report. No connection to audience values or aspirations whatsoever.

**Important nuance:** The appropriate level of emotional appeal varies by audience and context. A proposal to engineers should use lighter emotional touches than a consumer marketing campaign. Score based on whether the emotional register is appropriate for the audience, not whether it matches a consumer advertising standard.

## Scoring Calculation

### Step-by-Step Process

1. Assign each criterion a rating: PASS (100), CONCERN (60), or FAIL (0).
2. Multiply each score by its weight:
   - Audience Resonance: score x 0.30
   - Persuasive Structure: score x 0.25
   - Brand Tone: score x 0.20
   - Call-to-Action: score x 0.15
   - Emotional Connection: score x 0.10
3. Sum the weighted scores to get the total (0-100).

### Thresholds

| Range | Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 85-100 | Excellent | Highly persuasive, audience-ready |
| 70-84 | Good | Effective with minor persuasive gaps to address |
| 50-69 | Concerns | Weak persuasion, significant revision needed |
| 0-49 | Failing | Ineffective messaging, structural rewrite required |

### Worked Example

Given a document where:
- Audience Resonance: CONCERN (some "you" focus but frequent "we" language)
- Persuasive Structure: PASS (benefits lead, features support)
- Brand Tone: PASS (consistent professional tone throughout)
- Call-to-Action: CONCERN (CTA present but vague timeline)
- Emotional Connection: PASS (aspirational scenario in conclusion)

Calculation:
- 60 x 0.30 = 18
- 100 x 0.25 = 25
- 100 x 0.20 = 20
- 60 x 0.15 = 9
- 100 x 0.10 = 10
- **Total: 82** (Good -- effective with minor gaps)

The two CONCERN ratings (Audience Resonance and Call-to-Action) become the primary improvement targets.

```json
  "recommendations": [

```

**Rules for generating feedback:**

- **Strengths:** Cite specific passages or patterns from the document. Do not use generic praise.
- **Concerns:** Quote the problematic text and explain why it fails the criterion. Include counts or ratios where possible (e.g., "you/we ratio is 1:3").
- **Recommendations:** Every recommendation must include a priority level (CRITICAL, HIGH, OPTIONAL), a specific before/after transformation, and a brief reason. CRITICAL means it blocks effectiveness. HIGH means it significantly weakens persuasion. OPTIONAL means it would enhance but is not required.

## Common Improvement Patterns

When generating recommendations, use these proven transformations:

### Pattern 1: Flip Inside-Out Language
**Before:** "We provide an advanced CRM solution with comprehensive features."
**After:** "You get complete customer visibility in one dashboard -- no more hunting across spreadsheets."
**Technique:** Replace the subject (we/our) with the reader (you/your). Replace the abstract capability with the concrete outcome the reader experiences.

### Pattern 2: Lead with Benefits, Support with Features
**Before:** "Our system uses machine learning algorithms to process customer data."
**After:** "Cut customer onboarding time by 40% with automated data enrichment that learns your patterns."
**Technique:** State the measurable outcome first. Then name the mechanism in plain language. Connect the feature to the outcome with a causal link.

### Pattern 3: Strengthen the Call-to-Action
**Before:** "Please consider this proposal."
**After:** "Approve by March 15 to deploy before Q2 peak season and capture the $200K revenue window."
**Technique:** Add all four CTA components: action verb + specific action + deadline with reason + benefit of acting.

### Pattern 4: Add Emotional Connection
**Before:** "This system improves efficiency."
**After:** "Imagine your team building relationships instead of fighting spreadsheets -- that's the freedom this delivers."
**Technique:** Paint a before/after scenario the reader can visualize. Connect the rational benefit to an emotional value (freedom, control, growth).

### Pattern 5: Replace Corporate-Speak with Specifics
**Before:** "We leverage innovative synergies to optimize cross-functional alignment."
**After:** "Your sales and support teams share one customer view, so no one asks a customer to repeat themselves."
**Technique:** Ask "what does this actually mean for the reader?" and write that instead. If a phrase could appear in any company's marketing, it is too generic.

## Conflict Resolution

When marketing perspective conflicts with other stakeholder perspectives, resolve as follows:

| Conflict | Resolution |
|---|---|
| **Executive wants brevity, Marketing wants storytelling** | Lead with the brief ask, add the compelling narrative in the body. Both needs are met without compromise. |
| **Technical wants precision, Marketing wants emotional appeal** | Maintain technical accuracy. Layer persuasive language on top: use power words that are also truthful (validated, proven, measured). |
| **Legal wants hedging, Marketing wants bold claims** | Use confident but defensible language: "Designed to deliver" instead of "Guarantees". Frame hedges as credibility signals, not weakness. |
| **End-user wants simplicity, Marketing wants sophistication** | Simple language with aspirational framing. Short sentences, vivid outcomes, no jargon. |

**Priority rule:** When the deliverable type is marketing or sales material (campaign copy, sales deck, product page, promotional content), the marketing perspective takes priority in cases of unresolved conflict.
