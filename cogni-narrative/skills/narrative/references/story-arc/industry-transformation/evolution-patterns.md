## Structure Components

### New Industry Structure (300-400 words)

**What to extract:**
- Industry organization post-transformation
- Business model shifts
- Power redistribution (who gains/loses)
- Competitive dynamics changes
- Timeline to new equilibrium

**Questions to answer:**
- How will industry organize in future state?
- What business models thrive vs. struggle?
- Who holds power in new structure?
- How does competition work differently?
- When does new equilibrium stabilize?

**Example:**
```markdown
**New Industry Structure: Three-Layer Ecosystem (2027-2030)**

**Layer 1: Commoditized infrastructure (margin compression)**

Open-source foundation models (Llama, Mistral, Qwen families) plus cloud inference services compete on price and reliability, not capability<sup>[1]</sup>. Model performance converges at 92-95% on general tasks. Competition shifts from "best model" to "cheapest, most reliable inference."

**Market dynamics:** 10-15% gross margins (down from 60-70% for proprietary models), high volume required for profitability<sup>[2]</sup>. Winners: Hyperscalers with infrastructure scale (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) and well-funded open-source foundations with ecosystem lock-in. Losers: Pure-play model companies without differentiation (estimated 60-70% exit through acquisition or shutdown)<sup>[3]</sup>.

**Timeframe:** Commoditization largely complete by Q4 2025 - Q2 2026 as open-source reaches 95%+ parity<sup>[4]</sup>.

---

**Layer 2: Differentiated applications (value capture)**

Vertical-specific AI applications (healthcare diagnostics, financial fraud detection, legal research) capture majority of value through domain expertise, proprietary data, and regulatory compliance<sup>[5]</sup>. Applications built on commoditized infrastructure (Layer 1) but differentiate through:
- Proprietary training data (healthcare outcomes, financial transactions)
- Industry workflows and integrations (EHR systems, trading platforms, case management)
- Compliance infrastructure (HIPAA, SOX, EU AI Act certifications)<sup>[6]</sup>

**Market dynamics:** 40-60% gross margins through differentiation, pricing power from switching costs and domain moats<sup>[7]</sup>. Market fragments across verticals—no "winner-take-all," each vertical supports 3-5 significant players.

**Winners:** Established vertical software companies adding AI capabilities (Epic Systems in healthcare, Bloomberg in finance, LexisNexis in legal)<sup>[8]</sup>. These firms have data, workflows, compliance—adding AI to existing moats.

**Losers:** Horizontal AI platforms without vertical expertise. General chatbots and content generation commoditize (low margins), forcing pivot to verticals or exit<sup>[9]</sup>.

**Timeframe:** Vertical dominance establishes 2026-2028 as enterprises complete Layer 1 commoditization recognition and pivot budgets to Layer 2 differentiation<sup>[10]</sup>.

---

**Layer 3: Governance and orchestration (emerging category)**

AI governance platforms (model monitoring, bias detection, compliance automation, audit infrastructure) emerge as third value capture layer<sup>[11]</sup>. Organizations operating dozens to hundreds of AI models (mix of Layer 1 infrastructure, Layer 2 applications) require centralized governance.

**Market dynamics:** 35-50% gross margins, recurring revenue through operational necessity (compliance requirements, not optional)<sup>[12]</sup>. Market concentrates around 3-5 platforms (ModelOp, Fiddler, Arthur AI, new entrants) with strong network effects—more models governed = better anomaly detection = stickier platform.

**Winners:** Governance-first platforms establishing early enterprise deployments and regulatory compliance certifications. Platform effects favor first movers—organizations unlikely to multi-home across governance platforms<sup>[13]</sup>.

**Losers:** Late-entering governance vendors face established ecosystems and switching costs. DIY governance approaches collapse under complexity at scale (50+ models)<sup>[14]</sup>.

**Timeframe:** Governance layer crystallizes 2027-2029 as EU AI Act enforcement drives compliance requirement recognition and multi-model management complexity overwhelms manual approaches<sup>[15]</sup>.
```

### Power Shifts (100 words)

**What to extract:**
- Who gains power in new structure
- Who loses power
- Why power redistribution occurs

**Example:**
```markdown
**Power shifts from model developers to application specialists and governance providers<sup>[1]</sup>:**

**Gainers:**
- Vertical software incumbents (Epic, Bloomberg, Salesforce) with data, workflows, compliance
- Governance platforms capitalizing on regulatory requirements
- Infrastructure players with scale economics (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud)

**Losers:**
- Pure-play AI model companies without vertical or governance differentiation
- Horizontal AI platforms commoditized by open-source
- Consulting firms lacking specialized AI operational expertise<sup>[2]</sup>

Power concentrates where sustainable moats exist: proprietary data, regulatory compliance, operational orchestration<sup>[3]</sup>.
```


**Example:**
```markdown
**Winners in new structure:**

**Vertical software incumbents:** Already own customer relationships, proprietary data, compliance infrastructure<sup>[1]</sup>
- Add AI capabilities to existing workflows (evolutionary vs. revolutionary)
- Leverage 10-20 year customer switching costs
- Possess regulatory certifications AI-only vendors lack
**Examples:** Epic Systems (healthcare), Bloomberg Terminal (finance), Salesforce (CRM), Workday (HR)<sup>[2]</sup>

**Governance platform specialists:** First movers in emerging regulatory compliance category<sup>[3]</sup>
- Network effects from multi-model management
- Regulatory requirement moats (EU AI Act, future US rules)
- Platform stickiness (organizations unlikely to switch governance infrastructure)
**Examples:** ModelOp, Fiddler AI, Arthur AI, WhyLabs<sup>[4]</sup>

**Losers in new structure:**

**Pure-play LLM providers without vertical/governance strategy:** Commoditization eliminates model-only moats<sup>[5]</sup>
- Cannot compete on price against open-source
- Cannot compete on capability (parity achieved)
- Lack vertical domain expertise or governance differentiation
**Examples:** 60-70% of current AI model startups (estimated $20-40B in stranded VC investment)<sup>[6]</sup>

**Horizontal AI platforms (chatbots, content generation):** Commoditization crushes margins, no defensible positioning<sup>[7]</sup>
**Examples:** Generic chatbot providers, AI writing assistants without vertical specialization

**Transition challenge:** Model companies must pivot to verticals (acquire domain expertise + data) or governance (build compliance platform) within 18-24 months before commoditization completes. Most lack capital and time for successful pivot—consolidation through acquisition more likely than independent transformation<sup>[8]</sup>.
```

### Pattern 3: Business Model Evolution

**When to use:** Describing how value creation and capture mechanisms change

**Structure:**
```markdown
**Old business model (pre-transformation):**
- **Value creation:** [How value was created]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- **Value capture:** [How firms monetized]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- **Moats:** [Sources of competitive advantage]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**New business model (post-transformation):**
- **Value creation:** [How value is created]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- **Value capture:** [How firms monetize]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- **Moats:** [Sources of competitive advantage]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Transition implications:** [What organizations must change]<sup>[citation]</sup>
```

**Example:**
```markdown
**Old business model (2022-2024): Model capability differentiation**
- **Value creation:** Superior AI models (GPT-4 vs. GPT-3.5) created measurable customer value through capability gaps<sup>[1]</sup>
- **Value capture:** Usage-based pricing ($0.01-0.03 per 1K tokens), customers paid for better models<sup>[2]</sup>
- **Moats:** R&D investment ($1-10B model training), data scale, algorithmic expertise, talent concentration

**New business model (2026-2030): Application/governance differentiation**
- **Value creation:** Vertical applications and governance platforms create value through domain expertise, compliance, operational orchestration<sup>[3]</sup>—not model capability (commoditized)
- **Value capture:** Subscription + outcome-based pricing (e.g., $500K annually for healthcare AI + % of cost savings), customers pay for business results not API calls<sup>[4]</sup>
- **Moats:** Proprietary training data (healthcare outcomes, financial transactions), workflow integration depth (3-5 year customization), regulatory certifications (18-36 month lead time), operational expertise (tacit knowledge from managing 50+ model deployments)<sup>[5]</sup>

**Transition implications:**
Organizations must shift from:
- Model development → Application development + governance operations
- API pricing → SaaS + outcomes-based pricing
- Algorithm R&D → Domain expertise + compliance investment
- Talent acquisition (ML researchers) → Talent acquisition (industry experts + governance specialists)<sup>[6]</sup>

Organizations clinging to model-capability business models face 60-80% margin compression by 2026-2027 as commoditization completes<sup>[7]</sup>.
```

### Pattern 4: Timeline to New Equilibrium

**When to use:** Specifying when transformation stabilizes

**Structure:**
```markdown
**Phase 1 ([timeframe]): [Transition characteristic]**
[What happens in early phase]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Phase 2 ([timeframe]): [Transition characteristic]**
[What happens in middle phase]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Phase 3 ([timeframe]): [Transition characteristic]**
[What happens in mature phase]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Equilibrium ([timeframe]): [Stable state characteristics]**
[What stabilized structure looks like]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Uncertainty factors:** [What could accelerate/delay timeline]<sup>[citation]</sup>
```

**Example:**
```markdown
**Phase 1 (2024-2026): Commoditization recognition**
Open-source models reach 90-95% parity with proprietary alternatives<sup>[1]</sup>. Enterprises realize model differentiation unsustainable. AI spending shifts from model licensing to application development and governance infrastructure. Pure-play model companies face first consolidation wave (30-40% exit via acquisition or shutdown)<sup>[2]</sup>.

**Phase 2 (2026-2028): Vertical specialization dominance**
Horizontal AI platforms struggle with commoditized margins (10-15%) while vertical specialists thrive with differentiated pricing (40-60% margins)<sup>[3]</sup>. Market fragments across verticals—healthcare, finance, legal, manufacturing each develop 3-5 dominant AI application providers. Governance layer emerges as regulatory enforcement intensifies (EU AI Act, potential US federal rules)<sup>[4]</sup>.

**Phase 3 (2028-2030): Structure crystallization**
Three-layer ecosystem (infrastructure, applications, governance) solidifies<sup>[5]</sup>. Winners established, consolidation slows, competitive dynamics stabilize within layers. Infrastructure layer: 3-5 hyperscalers plus 2-3 major open-source foundations. Application layer: 15-25 significant players across verticals. Governance layer: 3-5 platforms dominating<sup>[6]</sup>.

**Equilibrium (2030+): Mature market characteristics**
AI industry resembles cloud computing structure: Commoditized infrastructure (AWS/Azure/GCP), differentiated applications (Salesforce, Workday, Epic), specialized tooling/governance (Datadog, Snowflake equivalents)<sup>[7]</sup>. Innovation shifts from foundational technology to vertical applications and use case expansion. Market growth continues but structural competition stable.

**Uncertainty factors:**
- **Regulatory fragmentation:** If US-EU diverge significantly (>40% requirement differences), could split into regional ecosystems, extending equilibrium to 2032-2035<sup>[8]</sup>
- **Breakthrough capabilities:** Unexpected AI capability jumps (e.g., true reasoning, multimodal mastery) could restart model differentiation, delaying commoditization 12-24 months<sup>[9]</sup>
- **Economic shock:** Major recession reducing AI budgets 30-50% could accelerate consolidation, bringing equilibrium forward to 2028-2029<sup>[10]</sup>
```

### Pattern 5: Competitive Dynamics Shifts

**When to use:** Explaining how competition mechanics change post-transformation

**Structure:**
```markdown
**Old competitive dynamics (pre-transformation):**
- [How firms competed]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- [Success factors]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- [Barriers to entry]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**New competitive dynamics (post-transformation):**
- [How firms compete]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- [Success factors]<sup>[citation]</sup>
- [Barriers to entry]<sup>[citation]</sup>

**Implication:** [What strategic shift requires]<sup>[citation]</sup>
```

**Example:**
```markdown
**Old competitive dynamics (2022-2024): Model capability arms race**
- **How firms competed:** R&D investment ($1-10B per model), larger training datasets (trillions of tokens), better algorithms, talent concentration (100+ PhD researchers)<sup>[1]</sup>
- **Success factors:** Capital access (VC/hyperscaler backing), technical expertise, first-to-market with capability improvements
- **Barriers to entry:** Extremely high ($5B+ realistic model training for frontier performance), but lowering as open-source narrows gap<sup>[2]</sup>

**New competitive dynamics (2026-2030): Domain/governance expertise depth**
- **How firms compete:** Industry-specific data quality (10+ years curated datasets), workflow integration depth (3-5 year customer customizations), compliance infrastructure sophistication (EU AI Act + sector regulations), operational orchestration at scale (managing 50-500 models)<sup>[3]</sup>
- **Success factors:** Domain expertise accumulation, customer relationship depth, regulatory/compliance readiness, operational excellence (less about research breakthrough)
- **Barriers to entry:** Moderate to high but different nature—time-based moats (3-5 years domain expertise, 2-3 years compliance infrastructure) vs. capital-based moats ($5B model training)<sup>[4]</sup>. New entrants can compete through superior vertical focus or governance innovation, not through better general models.

**Implication:** Strategic advantages shift from scale + capital to expertise + operational excellence<sup>[5]</sup>. Mid-market specialists can compete effectively against giants in specific verticals (healthcare AI startup can beat Google in hospital workflow integration despite 1000x size difference). Industry becomes more fragmented, less "winner-take-all" than pre-transformation expectations suggested<sup>[6]</sup>.
```

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ Mistake 1: Describing Change, Not End State

**Bad:**
> "The industry will transform significantly over the next few years."

**Why it fails:** Says it's changing but not what it becomes.

**Good:**
> "By 2027-2030, industry crystallizes as three-layer ecosystem: commoditized infrastructure (10-15% margins, 3-5 players), differentiated applications (40-60% margins, 15-25 vertical specialists), governance platforms (35-50% margins, 3-5 platforms)<sup>[1]</sup>."

**Why it works:** Describes specific end-state structure with quantification.

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: No Winners/Losers Identification

**Bad:**
> "The transformation will create opportunities for some and challenges for others."

**Why it fails:** Vague, doesn't identify who wins/loses or why.

**Good:**
> "Winners: Vertical incumbents (Epic, Bloomberg, Salesforce) with data + workflows, governance specialists (ModelOp, Fiddler) with compliance moats<sup>[1]</sup>. Losers: Pure-play model companies without differentiation (60-70% exit), horizontal platforms commoditized by open-source<sup>[2]</sup>."

**Why it works:** Names specific winners/losers with rationale.

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Missing Timeline to Stabilization

**Bad:**
> "Eventually, the industry will reach a new equilibrium."

**Why it fails:** No timeframe, can't plan for "eventually."

**Good:**
> "Equilibrium timeline: Commoditization recognition 2024-2026, vertical specialization dominance 2026-2028, structure crystallization 2028-2030<sup>[1]</sup>. Mature market characteristics stable by 2030+ unless regulatory fragmentation extends to 2032-2035<sup>[2]</sup>."

**Why it works:** Phased timeline with specific years and uncertainty factors.

---

### ❌ Mistake 4: Vague Business Model Description

**Bad:**
> "New business models will emerge based on AI capabilities."

**Why it fails:** What models specifically? How do they create/capture value?

**Good:**
> "Business model shifts from usage-based API pricing ($0.01-0.03/1K tokens) to subscription + outcomes ($500K annual + % cost savings)<sup>[1]</sup>. Value capture moves from model access to domain expertise, compliance infrastructure, operational orchestration<sup>[2]</sup>. Moats shift from R&D scale to proprietary data, workflow integration, regulatory certifications<sup>[3]</sup>."

**Why it works:** Specific pricing models, value sources, moat mechanisms.

---

### ❌ Mistake 5: No Competitive Dynamics Shift

**Bad:**
> "Competition will intensify as the industry transforms."

**Why it fails:** Doesn't explain how competition mechanics change.

**Good:**
> "Competitive dynamics shift from model capability arms race (success = $5B training budget + PhD talent) to domain expertise depth (success = 10+ year proprietary datasets + 3-5 year workflow integrations)<sup>[1]</sup>. Barriers to entry moderate from extremely high capital ($5B) to moderate time-based (3-5 years domain accumulation)<sup>[2]</sup>. Enables mid-market specialists to compete in verticals despite giant competitors."

**Why it works:** Contrasts old vs. new competition mechanics with specifics.

## Language Variations

### German Adjustments

**Structure description precision:**
- German business culture expects detailed organizational schemas
- Use hierarchical language: "Ebene 1," "Ebene 2," "Ebene 3"

**Economic quantification:**
- Include margins in both % and € where possible
- Specify market sizes (TAM) per layer/segment

**Timeline formality:**
- Use specific quarters and years
- "Q4 2025 - Q2 2026," not "in etwa 2 Jahre"

**Example (German style):**
```markdown
**Neue Industrie-Struktur: Drei-Ebenen-Ecosystem (2027-2030)**

**Ebene 1: Kommodifizierte Infrastruktur (Margin-Compression)**

Open-Source Foundation-Models (Llama, Mistral, Qwen Families) plus Cloud-Inference-Services konkurrieren auf Price und Reliability, nicht Capability<sup>[1]</sup>. Model-Performance konvergiert bei 92-95% auf General-Tasks. Wettbewerb shiftet von "best model" zu "cheapest, most reliable inference."

**Markt-Dynamiken:** 10-15% Gross-Margins (runter von 60-70% für Proprietary-Models), High-Volume erforderlich für Profitability<sup>[2]</sup>. Gewinner: Hyperscaler mit Infrastructure-Scale (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) und well-funded Open-Source-Foundations mit Ecosystem-Lock-in. Verlierer: Pure-Play Model-Companies ohne Differentiation (geschätzt 60-70% Exit durch Acquisition oder Shutdown, entspricht $20-40B stranded VC-Investment)<sup>[3]</sup>.

**Timeframe:** Commoditization größtenteils complete bis Q4 2025 - Q2 2026 sobald Open-Source 95%+ Parität erreicht<sup>[4]</sup>.

**Ökonomische Quantifizierung:** Ebene 1 Market-Size: $40-60B (2030), aufgeteilt: Cloud-Inference 70% ($28-42B), Open-Source-Foundations 30% ($12-18B, primär Support-Revenue)<sup>[5]</sup>.
```
